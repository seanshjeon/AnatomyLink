Transverse Atlantal Ligament

The transverse ligament of the atlas (TAL) is a strong horizontal band that holds the dens (odontoid process) of C2 against the anterior arch of the atlas (C1), preventing anterior atlantoaxial subluxation. It is the primary stabilizer of the atlantoaxial joint.

🔗 Key Connections

- Attachment — Lateral masses of atlas (C1) on each side - Holds in place — Dens of axis (C2) (Cervical Vertebrae) - Part of — Cruciate ligament complex (TAL + superior + inferior longitudinal bands) - Related — Alar Ligament, apical ligament, atlantoaxial joint, Spinal Cord

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Jefferson fracture (C1 burst fracture) — TAL rupture if lateral mass displacement > 7mm (Rule of Spence) → atlantoaxial instability - Rheumatoid arthritis — Pannus formation erodes TAL → atlantoaxial subluxation → cervical myelopathy - Down syndrome — TAL laxity → atlantoaxial instability; requires screening before contact sports - Odontoid fracture — Type II dens fracture associated with TAL injury - Open mouth (odontoid) X-ray — Assesses lateral mass displacement to evaluate TAL integrity

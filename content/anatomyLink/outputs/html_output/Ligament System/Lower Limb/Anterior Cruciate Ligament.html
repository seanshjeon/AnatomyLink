Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)

The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) runs from the anterior intercondylar area of the tibia to the lateral femoral condyle, preventing anterior tibial translation and rotational instability. It is one of the most commonly injured ligaments in sports.

🔗 Key Connections

- Origin — Anterior intercondylar area of Tibia (between tibial spines) - Insertion — Medial surface of lateral femoral condyle (Femur) - Two bundles — Anteromedial (AM, taut in flexion) + Posterolateral (PL, taut in extension) - Related structures — Posterior Cruciate Ligament, Medial Collateral Ligament (Knee), lateral meniscus, Femur, Tibia

🏥 Clinical Significance

- ACL tear — Common in cutting sports (soccer, basketball, skiing); "pop" + hemarthrosis + instability - Pivot shift test — Lateral tibial plateau subluxes anteriorly in extension, reduces in flexion - Anterior drawer test — Anterior tibial translation at 90° flexion - Lachman test — Most sensitive; anterior tibial translation at 20–30° flexion - ACL reconstruction — Patellar tendon (BTB), hamstring, or quadriceps tendon graft; no reliable healing when torn - Unhappy triad — ACL + MCL + medial meniscus (O'Donoghue); from valgus-rotation force

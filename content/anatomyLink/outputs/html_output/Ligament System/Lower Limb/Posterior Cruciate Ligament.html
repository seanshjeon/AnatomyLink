Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL)

The posterior cruciate ligament (PCL) is the strongest ligament in the knee, running from the posterior intercondylar area of the tibia to the medial femoral condyle. It prevents posterior tibial translation.

🔗 Key Connections

- Origin — Posterior intercondylar area of Tibia (posterior to tibial spines) - Insertion — Lateral surface of medial femoral condyle (Femur) - Two bundles — Anterolateral (larger, taut in flexion) + Posteromedial (taut in extension) - Related structures — Anterior Cruciate Ligament, menisci, Femur, Tibia

🏥 Clinical Significance

- PCL tear — Dashboard injury (knee flexed, tibia pushed posterior); posterior "sag" of tibia - Posterior sag sign — Tibia sags posteriorly when knee held at 90° flexion - Posterior drawer test — Posterior tibial translation at 90° flexion - PCL reconstruction — Less common than ACL; often managed conservatively if isolated tear - Dial test — Increased external tibial rotation at 30° (PLC) vs 90° (PCL + PLC) indicates injury level

Lateral Collateral Ligament (Knee)

The lateral collateral ligament (LCL) (fibular collateral ligament) resists varus stress at the knee, connecting the lateral femoral condyle to the fibular head. It is part of the posterolateral corner (PLC) complex.

🔗 Key Connections

- Origin — Lateral femoral condyle (Femur) - Insertion — Fibular Head - Does NOT attach — Lateral meniscus (unlike MCL and medial meniscus) - PLC structures — LCL + popliteus tendon + popliteofibular ligament + posterolateral capsule - Related structures — Common Fibular Nerve (wraps around fibular head nearby!)

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Varus stress test — At 0° and 30° flexion; lateral gapping - PLC injury — LCL often injured as part of posterolateral corner complex; combined with ACL or PCL - Peroneal nerve injury — Common Fibular Nerve at risk with LCL/PLC injuries → foot drop - Dial test — Increased external rotation at 30° flexion suggests PLC injury - Rarely isolated — LCL injury usually occurs with PLC or cruciate injuries

Posterior Longitudinal Ligament (PLL)

The posterior longitudinal ligament (PLL) runs along the posterior surface of the vertebral bodies (inside the spinal canal) from C2 to the sacrum. It resists hyperflexion and helps contain disc herniations.

🔗 Key Connections

- Attachment — Posterior surface of vertebral bodies and discs (C2 → Sacrum), inside the spinal canal - Related structures — Intervertebral Disc, Spinal Cord, Vertebral Column - Counterpart — Anterior Longitudinal Ligament

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Disc herniation — PLL is thicker centrally; herniations often occur posterolaterally where PLL is thinner - OPLL (Ossification of PLL) — Common in East Asian populations; causes cervical myelopathy by compressing spinal cord - Hyperflexion injury — PLL rupture in flexion-distraction injuries (seat belt injuries)

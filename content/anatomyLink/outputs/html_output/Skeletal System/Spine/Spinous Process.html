Spinous Process

The spinous process is the posterior bony projection of each vertebra, formed by the fusion of the two laminae. It is palpable as the midline "bumps" of the back and serves as an attachment for back muscles and ligaments.

🔗 Key Connections

- Vertebral Column — posterior element of each vertebra - Cervical Vertebrae — C2, C7 (vertebra prominens) most prominent; C3–C6 bifid - Thoracic Vertebrae — long, downward-sloping spinous processes - Lumbar Vertebrae — broad, horizontal spinous processes - Trapezius — attaches to spinous processes (ligamentum nuchae in cervical spine) - Erector Spinae — broad muscle group along spinous processes - Interspinous ligament — connects adjacent spinous processes - Supraspinous ligament — runs over the tips of spinous processes

🗂️ Key Facts

Region Spinous Process Characteristics
Cervical Bifid (C3–C6); C7 = vertebra prominens (most palpable)
Thoracic Long, steeply angled downward
Lumbar Broad, horizontal — "hatchet" shape

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Spinous process fracture — "Clay-shoveler's fracture": avulsion of C6/C7/T1 spinous process - Tenderness on palpation — Discitis, vertebral fracture, or ligamentous injury - Epidural injection landmarks — Spinous processes guide needle placement (midline approach) - Vertebra prominens (C7) — Used as anatomical landmark to count vertebrae

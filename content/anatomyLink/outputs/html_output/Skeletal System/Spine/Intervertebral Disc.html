Intervertebral Disc

The intervertebral disc is a fibrocartilaginous structure between adjacent vertebral bodies in the Vertebral Column, acting as a shock absorber and allowing spinal flexibility. It consists of an outer annulus fibrosus and an inner nucleus pulposus.

🔗 Key Connections

- Vertebral Column — located between each vertebral body (C2–3 to L5–S1) - Spinal Cord — disc herniations compress adjacent nerve roots or the spinal cord - Cervical Vertebrae — cervical discs; most mobile region - Lumbar Vertebrae — lumbar discs; highest load; most commonly herniated (L4–L5, L5–S1)

🗂️ Anatomy

Component Structure Function
Annulus fibrosus Concentric collagen rings Contain nucleus; resist torsion
Nucleus pulposus Gelatinous core (notochord remnant) Distribute compressive forces
End plates Cartilaginous; superior + inferior Nutrient diffusion (avascular disc)

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Disc herniation — Nucleus pulposus extrudes through annular tear → nerve root compression - L4–L5: L5 root → dorsiflexion weakness, lateral leg/dorsal foot numbness - L5–S1: S1 root → plantarflexion weakness, lateral foot numbness, reduced ankle reflex - Discogenic back pain — Annular tears causing axial low back pain without radiculopathy - Disc degeneration — Loss of water content → disc height loss → foraminal narrowing → stenosis - Cauda equina syndrome — Massive central herniation at L1–L2 or below → surgical emergency

📍 Location

Skeletal System → Vertebral Column → Intervertebral Disc

Canines

Canines (cuspids) are the 4 pointed teeth (2 upper, 2 lower) at the corners of the dental arch, designed for tearing and piercing food. They have the longest roots of any tooth and are the most stable teeth in the mouth.

🗂️ Key Features

Property Detail
Count 4 (teeth #6, #11 upper; #22, #27 lower)
Crown Single cusp (pointed); chisel-like
Root Single; longest root in the mouth
Eruption 9–12 years (last anterior tooth to erupt)
Deciduous "Baby fangs" erupt at 16–23 months

🔑 Key Functions

- Tearing food — Primary function with the pointed cusp - Canine guidance — During lateral jaw movements, upper canines guide the lower jaw (canine-protected occlusion); prevents wear on posterior teeth - Arch corner — Anchors the curvature of the dental arch

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Longest retention — Due to long roots, canines are often the last teeth lost in edentulous patients - Palpation — Upper canine root can be palpated through the cheek just below the eye socket (hence "eye tooth")

🔗 Key Connections

- Teeth — 4 of 32 permanent teeth - Mandible — lower canines (#22, #27) - Skull — upper canines (#6, #11) in maxilla - Cranial Nerves — CN V2 (upper) + CN V3 (lower) innervation

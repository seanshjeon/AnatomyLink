Premolars (Bicuspids)

Premolars (bicuspids) are the 8 transitional teeth between canines and molars (4 upper, 4 lower), designed for crushing and tearing food. They are absent in deciduous dentition and only appear with permanent teeth.

🗂️ Types

Type Location Count Roots
1st premolars Adjacent to canine 4 Upper: usually 2; Lower: 1
2nd premolars Between 1st PM and 1st molar 4 1 (both upper and lower)

Universal numbers: Upper #4, #5, #12, #13 / Lower #20, #21, #28, #29

🔑 Key Anatomy

- Cusps — Two cusps (buccal + lingual/palatal) → hence "bicuspid" - Upper 1st premolar — Most commonly has 2 roots (buccal + palatal) and 2 canals - Occlusion — Intercuspation with opposing premolars during chewing

🔑 Premolar Extraction in Orthodontics

Upper first premolars are the most commonly extracted teeth in orthodontic treatment to create space for retracting crowded or protruding anterior teeth.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Extraction for braces — Upper 1st premolars commonly extracted to relieve crowding - Buccal furcation — Upper 1st premolar furcation (two roots) can be difficult to clean → early periodontitis - Mental foramen — Located near the apex of lower 1st–2nd premolars; risk of mental nerve injury during extraction - Premolar occlusion — Premature contact here → lateral jaw deviation and TMJ strain

🔗 Key Connections

- Teeth — 8 of 32 permanent teeth; absent in deciduous dentition - Mandible — lower premolars (#20, #21, #28, #29) - Skull — upper premolars (#4, #5, #12, #13) in maxilla - Cranial Nerves — CN V2 (upper, via middle superior alveolar nerve) + CN V3 (lower, via inferior alveolar nerve)

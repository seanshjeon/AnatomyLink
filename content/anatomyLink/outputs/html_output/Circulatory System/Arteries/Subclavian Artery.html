Subclavian Artery

The subclavian artery is the primary arterial supply to the upper limb, arising from the brachiocephalic trunk (right) or Aorta directly (left), passing over the first Ribs and becoming the axillary artery at the lateral border of the first rib.

🔗 Key Connections

- Arises from: Aorta (left) / brachiocephalic trunk (right) - Continues as: axillary artery → Brachial Artery - Key branch: vertebral artery → Brain (posterior circulation) - Passes over: first rib (Ribs) — landmark for name change - Relation to: Clavicle (passes beneath), Scapula (via suprascapular artery)

🗂️ Key Branches

Branch Supply
Vertebral artery Ascends through cervical transverse foramina (Cervical Vertebrae) → basilar artery → posterior Brain
Internal mammary (thoracic) artery Anterior thoracic wall; used as coronary bypass graft for LAD
Thyrocervical trunk Thyroid (inferior thyroid a.), Scapula (suprascapular a.), neck
Costocervical trunk Posterior upper intercostals; deep cervical muscles

🔑 Name Changes of the Upper Limb Artery

Subclavian → Axillary (at lateral border of 1st rib) → Brachial (at lower border of teres major) → Radial Artery + Ulnar Artery (at cubital fossa)

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Subclavian steal syndrome — proximal subclavian stenosis → retrograde vertebral flow → posterior circulation ischemia with arm exercise (dizziness, diplopia, drop attacks) - Thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS) — compression between clavicle and first rib → arm pain, pallor, weakness; can compress Brachial Plexus (neurogenic TOS, most common) or subclavian artery/vein - Subclavian line — central venous access; risk of pneumothorax (lung apex nearby) - Blood pressure measurement — BP measured in brachial artery; difference >20 mmHg between arms suggests subclavian stenosis

Celiac Trunk

The celiac trunk is the first unpaired visceral branch of the abdominal Aorta, arising at T12 just below the diaphragm and supplying the foregut (stomach, proximal duodenum, liver, gallbladder, spleen, pancreas).

🔗 Key Connections

- Arises from: Aorta (at T12) - Supplies: foregut derivatives

🗂️ Three Main Branches

Branch Supply
Left gastric artery Lesser curvature of stomach; esophago-gastric junction
Splenic artery Spleen, pancreas (multiple branches), greater curvature of stomach (via short gastric and left gastroepiploic aa.)
Common hepatic artery Liver (via proper hepatic a.), gallbladder (cystic a.), stomach (right gastric a.), duodenum (gastroduodenal a. → superior pancreaticoduodenal + right gastroepiploic)

Mnemonic: Left Side Has (Left gastric, Splenic, Hepatic)

🔑 Gastroduodenal Artery

The gastroduodenal artery (branch of common hepatic) passes posterior to the first part of the duodenum — a clinically crucial relationship, as posterior duodenal ulcers erode into it causing severe haemorrhage.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Celiac artery stenosis / compression — median arcuate ligament syndrome; post-prandial angina (mesenteric ischaemia) - Gastroduodenal artery haemorrhage — posterior duodenal ulcer erosion → massive upper GI bleed; angiographic embolisation or surgical ligation - Splenic artery aneurysm — most common visceral artery aneurysm; rupture risk in pregnancy - Hepatic artery in surgery — must be identified in Pringle's manoeuvre (hepatic inflow occlusion) during liver surgery

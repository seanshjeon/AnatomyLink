Femoral Artery

The femoral artery is the main arterial supply of the lower limb, continuing from the external iliac artery at the inguinal ligament and descending through the femoral triangle and adductor canal to become the Popliteal Artery at the adductor hiatus.

🔗 Key Connections

- Continues from: Iliac Arteries (external iliac, at inguinal ligament) - Continues as: Popliteal Artery (at adductor hiatus / Femur distal) - Accompanies: Femoral Nerve (lateral in femoral triangle) - Runs through: femoral triangle → adductor canal → adductor hiatus

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Inguinal ligament (continuation of external iliac)
Termination Adductor hiatus in adductor magnus → Popliteal Artery
Key Branch Profunda femoris (deep femoral artery) — largest branch; supplies Quadriceps Femoris, Hamstrings, adductors; medial circumflex femoral artery → femoral head blood supply
Pulse Femoral pulse: mid-inguinal point (midpoint between ASIS and pubic symphysis)

🔑 Femoral Triangle Contents (lateral to medial)

VAN — Vein, Artery, Nerve (femoral vein medial to artery; Femoral Nerve lateral to artery)

🔑 Femoral Head Blood Supply

The medial circumflex femoral artery (branch of profunda femoris) is the main supply to the femoral head via the retinacular vessels. Disrupted in femoral neck fractures → avascular necrosis of the femoral head.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) — atherosclerosis → intermittent claudication (thigh/calf) → rest pain → ulceration/gangrene; ABI <0.9 diagnostic - Femoral artery aneurysm — pulsatile groin mass; often associated with popliteal aneurysm - Femoral access — cardiac catheterization, angiography, TAVI; risk of haematoma, pseudoaneurysm, AV fistula - Femoral pseudoaneurysm — arterial blood leaks into a tissue cavity (post-procedure); pulsatile swelling; treated with ultrasound-guided compression or thrombin injection - Femoral vein — DVT — femoral DVT → PE risk; DVT propagates from calf veins

Aorta

The aorta is the largest artery in the body, arising from the left ventricle of the Heart and distributing oxygenated blood to the entire systemic circulation. It has four named segments.

🔗 Key Connections

- Arises from: Heart (left ventricle, aortic valve) - First branches: Coronary Arteries (from aortic sinuses) - Major branches: Carotid Arteries, Subclavian Artery, Celiac Trunk, Renal Arteries, Iliac Arteries - Runs along: Thoracic Vertebrae, Lumbar Vertebrae

🗂️ Four Segments

Segment Location Key Branches
Ascending aorta From aortic valve → aortic arch Coronary Arteries (L & R)
Aortic arch Superior mediastinum Brachiocephalic trunk → Carotid Arteries + Subclavian Artery (right); Left Carotid Arteries; Left Subclavian Artery
Descending thoracic aorta T4 → T12 (diaphragm) Intercostal arteries (T3–T11), bronchial arteries
Abdominal aorta T12 → L4 (bifurcation) Celiac Trunk (T12), SMA (L1), Renal Arteries (L1–L2), IMA (L3), Iliac Arteries (L4)

🔑 Aortic Arch Branches (Left to Right — Memorize)

1. Brachiocephalic trunk (innominate) → right common carotid + right subclavian 2. Left common carotid 3. Left subclavian

🔑 Abdominal Aorta Branches (Unpaired visceral)

- Celiac trunk (T12) — Celiac Trunk: stomach, spleen, liver - Superior mesenteric artery (SMA, L1) — small bowel + proximal colon - Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA, L3) — distal colon + rectum

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Aortic aneurysm — abnormal dilatation >3 cm (AAA) or >4.5 cm (thoracic) - AAA: most common in infrarenal aorta; pulsatile abdominal mass; rupture → catastrophic hemorrhage - Risk: male, elderly, smoking, hypertension, family history - Screening: abdominal USS in men >65 who have ever smoked - Aortic dissection — intimal tear → blood enters media → two lumens - Type A (ascending) — surgical emergency; risk of coronary/aortic valve/brain involvement - Type B (descending only) — medical management (anti-impulse therapy) - Classic: sudden "tearing" chest/back pain; unequal arm blood pressures - Coarctation of the aorta — congenital narrowing, usually at ligamentum arteriosum (just distal to left subclavian) - Upper body hypertension, lower body hypotension; rib notching on CXR - Associated with Turner syndrome, bicuspid aortic valve - Aortitis — inflammation; causes: Takayasu's arteritis, giant cell arteritis, syphilis

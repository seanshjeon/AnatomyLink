Popliteal Artery

The popliteal artery is the continuation of the Femoral Artery through the popliteal fossa, behind the knee, before dividing into the anterior tibial artery and the tibioperoneal trunk (→ posterior tibial + fibular arteries).

🔗 Key Connections

- Continues from: Femoral Artery (at adductor hiatus) - Divides into: anterior tibial artery + tibioperoneal trunk (posterior tibial + fibular arteries) - Relates to: Femur (posterior, in popliteal fossa), Tibia (posterior, at lower border of popliteus) - Accompanies: tibial nerve (Tibial Nerve) — most medial in popliteal fossa - Overlying: Gastrocnemius heads

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Adductor hiatus in Femur
Termination Lower border of popliteus → anterior tibial artery + tibioperoneal trunk
Branches Genicular arteries (×5 — supply knee joint); sural arteries (supply Gastrocnemius, soleus)
Pulse Popliteal pulse: deep in popliteal fossa — patient prone, knee slightly flexed; press firmly

🔑 Popliteal Fossa Contents (Surface to Deep)

Biceps femoris (lateral), semitendinosus/membranosus (medial); then superficial to deep: tibial nerve → popliteal vein → popliteal artery (deepest, next to bone)

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Popliteal artery aneurysm — most common peripheral artery aneurysm; often bilateral; presents with claudication, thrombosis, or distal embolisation; 50% associated with AAA - Popliteal artery entrapment syndrome — young athletes; fibromuscular band compresses artery → exertional claudication; abnormal popliteal artery course around medial head of Gastrocnemius - Knee dislocation — popliteal artery injury in ~30% of posterior dislocations; must assess vascular status and consider angiography/CT angiogram - Genicular arteries — extensive anastomotic network around knee; collateral supply if femoral artery occluded slowly - Below-knee amputation level — popliteal vessel status determines feasibility

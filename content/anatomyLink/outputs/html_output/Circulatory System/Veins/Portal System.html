Portal System

The hepatic portal system is a venous circuit carrying nutrient-rich blood from the gastrointestinal tract and spleen to the liver before it returns to the systemic circulation. It is unique in that it connects two capillary beds (gut → liver sinusoids).

🔗 Key Connections

- Drains: stomach, small intestine, large intestine, spleen, pancreas - Delivers to: liver (hepatic sinusoids) - Empties via: hepatic veins → Inferior Vena Cava → Heart - Anastomoses with: systemic veins at portosystemic anastomoses

🗂️ Formation of the Portal Vein

Tributary Drains
Superior mesenteric vein (SMV) Small bowel + proximal colon
Splenic vein Spleen + pancreas + inferior mesenteric vein (distal colon, rectum)
SMV + Splenic vein unite behind neck of pancreas → Portal vein
Portal vein ascends in hepatoduodenal ligament (with hepatic artery + common bile duct) → right and left branches into liver

🔑 Portosystemic Anastomoses

When portal pressure rises (portal hypertension), blood is shunted into systemic veins at these sites:

Site Portal Vein Systemic Vein Clinical Result
Lower oesophagus Left gastric vein Azygos vein Oesophageal varices — rupture → massive haematemesis
Rectum Superior rectal vein Middle/inferior rectal veins Rectal varices (not haemorrhoids)
Umbilicus Paraumbilical veins Superficial epigastric veins Caput medusae — dilated veins radiating from umbilicus
Bare area of liver Hepatic veins Diaphragmatic veins Retroperitoneal collaterals

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Portal hypertension — portal pressure >12 mmHg (normal 5–10 mmHg); causes: cirrhosis (most common in West), portal vein thrombosis, schistosomiasis - Consequences: oesophageal varices, splenomegaly, ascites, caput medusae, hepatic encephalopathy - Oesophageal varices — rupture is a medical emergency; mortality 15–20% per bleed; managed with terlipressin + endoscopic band ligation / sclerotherapy - TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) — creates artificial connection between portal and hepatic veins to reduce portal pressure - Budd-Chiari syndrome — hepatic vein occlusion → blocked portal outflow → acute liver failure; triad: abdominal pain, hepatomegaly, ascites - Portal vein thrombosis — abdominal pain + splenomegaly; anticoagulation; causes: hypercoagulable states, pancreatitis, cirrhosis

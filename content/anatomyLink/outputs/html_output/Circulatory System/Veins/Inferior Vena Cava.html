Inferior Vena Cava

The inferior vena cava (IVC) is the largest vein in the body, returning deoxygenated blood from the lower body (abdomen, pelvis, lower limbs) to the right atrium of the Heart. It lies to the right of the Aorta.

🔗 Key Connections

- Drains into: Heart (right atrium) - Formed by: union of right and left common iliac veins (at L5, right of Aorta bifurcation)

🗂️ Key Tributaries

Tributary Level Drains
Common iliac veins L5 Pelvis + lower limbs
Lumbar veins L1–L4 Posterior abdominal wall
Right gonadal vein L2 Right testis/ovary (left gonadal → left renal vein)
Renal veins L1–L2 Kidneys
Right suprarenal vein Above renal Right adrenal (left → left renal vein)
Hepatic veins (×3) T8 Liver (via Portal System)

🔑 Left-Sided Asymmetry

- Left gonadal vein → left renal vein → IVC (not directly into IVC) - Left suprarenal vein → left renal vein → IVC - Left-sided IVC: rare congenital variant; important in retroperitoneal surgery

🏥 Clinical Significance

- DVT → Pulmonary embolism — thrombus from femoral/iliac veins propagates to IVC → pulmonary arteries - IVC filter — placed in infrarenal IVC to trap emboli; indicated when anticoagulation contraindicated or fails - IVC obstruction — Budd-Chiari syndrome (hepatic vein/IVC occlusion → liver congestion → ascites, jaundice, RUQ pain); IVC compression by tumour (renal cell carcinoma — "tumour thrombus" extends into IVC) - Renal cell carcinoma — can invade right renal vein and extend as tumour thrombus into IVC and even right atrium

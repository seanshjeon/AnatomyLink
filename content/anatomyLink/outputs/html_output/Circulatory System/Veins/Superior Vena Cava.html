Superior Vena Cava

The superior vena cava (SVC) is the large venous trunk returning deoxygenated blood from the upper body (head, neck, upper limbs, thorax) to the right atrium of the Heart. It has no valves.

🔗 Key Connections

- Drains into: Heart (right atrium) - Formed by: right and left brachiocephalic veins (each formed by subclavian + internal jugular veins) - Drains: Jugular Veins (head/neck), subclavian veins (upper limbs), azygos system (posterior thorax) - Relation to: Sternum (behind manubrium), ascending Aorta (to the left), right lung hilum

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Formed by Right + left brachiocephalic veins (behind right 1st costal cartilage)
Length ~7 cm; enters right atrium at the level of the 3rd costal cartilage
Tributaries Azygos vein (drains posterior intercostals), brachiocephalic veins
No valves Blood flows by negative intrathoracic pressure

🔑 Azygos System

The azygos vein (right) and hemiazygos veins (left) drain the posterior thoracic wall, esophagus, and bronchi → empty into the SVC. The azygos is a crucial collateral pathway if the SVC or IVC is obstructed.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- SVC syndrome — obstruction of SVC → facial/arm swelling, dilated neck veins, headache, cyanosis - Most common cause: lung cancer (right-sided) or lymphoma compressing SVC - Other causes: thrombosis from central lines/pacemakers - Collateral veins visible on chest wall; reversed flow in jugular veins - Central venous catheter (CVC) — tip should lie in SVC/cavoatrial junction for accurate CVP monitoring - Pacemaker leads — traverse SVC to right ventricle

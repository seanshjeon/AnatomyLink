Pancreas

The pancreas is a retroperitoneal mixed exocrine/endocrine gland, lying posterior to the Stomach at the L1–L2 level. Its exocrine function (digestive enzymes) and endocrine function (insulin, glucagon) make it essential for digestion and glucose homeostasis.

🔗 Key Connections

- Location: retroperitoneal, behind Stomach, in front of Lumbar Vertebrae (L1–L2) - Head sits in: C-loop of duodenum (Small Intestine) - Blood supply: Celiac Trunk (splenic artery → body/tail) + SMA (inferior pancreaticoduodenal → head) - Venous drainage: splenic vein + SMV → Portal System - Duct: main pancreatic duct (Wirsung) joins common bile duct → opens at ampulla of Vater (sphincter of Oddi) in 2nd part of duodenum - Relations: Inferior Vena Cava, Aorta, portal vein (posterior to neck); spleen (tail touches)

Part Location Key Relationship
Head Within duodenal C-loop Uncinate process wraps behind SMA; CBD runs through

🗂️ Exocrine vs. Endocrine

Division Cells Product Function
Exocrine (80%) Acinar cells Digestive enzymes (amylase, lipase, proteases — trypsin/chymotrypsin) Digestion in Small Intestine
Exocrine Ductal cells HCO₃⁻-rich fluid (secretin-stimulated) Neutralises gastric acid in duodenum
Endocrine (2%) — islets of Langerhans α cells Glucagon Raises blood glucose
β cells (most common) Insulin Lowers blood glucose; promotes storage
δ cells Somatostatin Inhibits both α and β cells
PP cells Pancreatic polypeptide Inhibits pancreatic secretion

🔑 Enzyme Activation — Trypsinogen Cascade

Pancreatic proteases are secreted as inactive zymogens to prevent autodigestion. Trypsinogen → trypsin (by enterokinase/enteropeptidase on duodenal brush border) → activates chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, phospholipase A2. Failure of this control → acute pancreatitis.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Acute pancreatitis — activated enzymes digest pancreas itself; causes: gallstones (45%) + alcohol (35%); Ranson/Glasgow criteria for severity; amylase/lipase elevated; treat: NBM, IV fluids, analgesia; complications: pseudocyst, necrosis, ARDS, multi-organ failure - Chronic pancreatitis — progressive fibrosis; alcohol is main cause; chronic pain + malabsorption (steatorrhoea) + diabetes; "chain of lakes" + calcifications on CT - Pancreatic cancer — 3rd most common GI cancer; 85% ductal adenocarcinoma; head of pancreas (most common) → obstructive jaundice + Courvoisier's sign (palpable non-tender GB); poor prognosis (5-year survival ~12%); treat: Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) if resectable; CA 19-9 marker - Insulinoma — most common functioning islet cell tumour; Whipple's triad: symptoms with fasting, glucose <2.2 mmol/L, relief with glucose; treat: surgical enucleation - Glucagonoma — rare α-cell tumour; necrolytic migratory erythema + diabetes + weight loss - VIPoma (Verner-Morrison) — watery diarrhoea + hypokalaemia + achlorhydria (WDHA syndrome)

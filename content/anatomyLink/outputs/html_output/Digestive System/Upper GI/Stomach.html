Stomach

The stomach is a J-shaped muscular organ in the left upper quadrant, serving as a reservoir and initiating protein digestion via gastric acid (HCl) and pepsinogen. It can hold ~1–1.5 litres.

🔗 Key Connections

- Receives from: Esophagus (via gastro-oesophageal junction) - Empties into: Small Intestine (duodenum, via pylorus) - Blood supply: all from Celiac Trunk - Lesser curvature: left gastric + right gastric arteries - Greater curvature: left + right gastroepiploic arteries - Fundus: short gastric arteries (from splenic artery) - Venous drainage: → Portal System - Relations: liver (right), spleen (left), Pancreas (posterior), transverse colon (inferior)

🗂️ Regions

Region Location Key Feature
Cardia Near GO junction Receives food from Esophagus
Fundus Superior dome Gas bubble on X-ray; short gastric arteries
Body Main region Rugae (folds for expansion); parietal cells (HCl + intrinsic factor); chief cells (pepsinogen)
Antrum Distal G cells (gastrin); mixes food; antral pump
Pylorus Junction with duodenum Pyloric sphincter (muscular); controls gastric emptying

🗂️ Gastric Secretory Cells

Cell Type Location Secretion Function
Parietal (oxyntic) Body / fundus HCl + intrinsic factor Protein denaturation; B12 absorption
Chief (peptic) Body / fundus Pepsinogen Converted to pepsin by HCl → protein digestion
G cells Antrum Gastrin Stimulates parietal cells; increases motility
Mucous neck cells Throughout Mucus + HCO₃⁻ Mucosal protection
Enterochromaffin-like (ECL) Body Histamine Stimulates parietal cells

🔑 Gastric Acid Regulation

- Stimulated by: gastrin (G cells), histamine (ECL cells), acetylcholine (vagus) - Inhibited by: somatostatin, secretin (duodenal acidification), CCK - Proton pump (H⁺/K⁺-ATPase) on parietal cells — target of PPIs (omeprazole)

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) — gastric or duodenal ulcer; causes: H. pylori (70%), NSAIDs, excess acid - Gastric ulcer: pain worse with eating; weight loss; malignancy risk → biopsy all

- Gastroparesis — delayed gastric emptying; causes: diabetes (autonomic neuropathy), post-vagotomy - Pyloric stenosis — infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis; projectile non-bilious vomiting at 2–8 weeks; olive-shaped mass in epigastrium; treat: Ramstedt's pyloromyotomy - Pernicious anaemia — autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → no intrinsic factor → B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anaemia + subacute combined degeneration of spinal cord - Zollinger-Ellison syndrome — gastrin-secreting tumour (gastrinoma) → massive acid production → recurrent severe peptic ulcers + diarrhoea

Sternocleidomastoid (SCM)

The SCM is the largest and most superficial muscle of the neck, forming the anterior-lateral boundary of the posterior triangle. It is the key muscle for neck rotation and flexion.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Sternum (manubrium — sternal head) + Clavicle (medial 1/3 — clavicular head) - Insertion: Skull (mastoid process + superior nuchal line of occipital bone)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Manubrium (Sternum) + medial Clavicle
Insertion Mastoid process + superior nuchal line (Skull)
Action Bilateral: neck flexion; Unilateral: ipsilateral lateral flexion + contralateral rotation
Innervation Spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) + C2–C3 (proprioception)
Blood Supply Occipital artery, superior thyroid artery

🔑 Anatomical Triangles

The SCM separates: - Anterior triangle (medial to SCM) - Posterior triangle (lateral to SCM)

The spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) runs through the posterior triangle → innervates the Trapezius.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Torticollis (Wry neck) — Unilateral SCM spasm or fibrosis; head tilts ipsilaterally, chin rotates contralaterally - Congenital torticollis — SCM fibrosis from birth injury - CN XI injury — During posterior triangle surgery → SCM and trapezius weakness - Cervical lymph nodes — Run along the deep surface of SCM; enlarged in infection/malignancy

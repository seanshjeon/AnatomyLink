Gastrocnemius

The gastrocnemius is the large, two-headed superficial calf muscle forming the rounded contour of the posterior lower leg. It is a powerful plantarflexor and assists in knee flexion.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Femur (medial head: medial femoral condyle; lateral head: lateral femoral condyle) — posterior surfaces - Insertion: Tarsals (posterior calcaneus — calcaneal tuberosity) via the Achilles tendon (shared with soleus)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Medial & lateral femoral condyles (Femur)
Insertion Calcaneal tuberosity (Tarsals) via Achilles tendon
Action Ankle plantarflexion (powerful); knee flexion (when foot is free)
Innervation Tibial Nerve (S1, S2)
Blood Supply Sural branches of popliteal artery

🔑 Achilles Tendon (Calcaneal Tendon)

Formed by the gastrocnemius + soleus tendons. The strongest and thickest tendon in the body. Inserts on the posterior calcaneus (calcaneal tuberosity).

🔑 Gastrocnemius vs. Soleus

Feature Gastrocnemius Soleus
Heads Two (medial, lateral) One
Crosses Knee + ankle Ankle only
Fiber type Fast-twitch (power) Slow-twitch (endurance)
Origin Femoral condyles Posterior tibia/fibula

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Achilles tendon rupture — Most common mechanism: sudden push-off (e.g., squash, basketball); Thompson test positive (no plantarflexion on calf squeeze) - Achilles tendinopathy — Overuse; mid-portion or insertional - Gastrocnemius strain ("Tennis leg") — Acute medial head tear; sudden calf pain - Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) — The soleal venous plexus and posterior tibial veins lie adjacent to gastrocnemius - Ankle reflex (S1) — Tested by tapping the Achilles tendon; gastrocnemius and soleus both involved

Iliopsoas

The iliopsoas is the most powerful hip flexor, formed by the union of two muscles: iliacus and psoas major. It is essential for walking, running, and sitting up.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: - Psoas major: Lumbar Vertebrae (vertebral bodies T12–L5 and transverse processes) - Iliacus: Hip Bone (iliac fossa) - Insertion: Femur (lesser trochanter)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Lumbar Vertebrae (psoas) + iliac fossa (Hip Bone) (iliacus)
Insertion Lesser trochanter of Femur
Action Hip flexion (primary); external rotation; trunk flexion when hip is fixed
Innervation Psoas major: anterior rami L1–L3; Iliacus: Femoral Nerve (L2–L4)
Blood Supply Lumbar arteries (psoas); iliac branches (iliacus)

🔑 Anatomical Course

The iliopsoas runs from the abdomen/pelvis, under the inguinal ligament, to insert on the lesser trochanter. It is separated from the hip joint only by the iliopectineal bursa.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Iliopsoas abscess — Psoas abscess from vertebral osteomyelitis (TB, Staphylococcus); flank/groin pain + fever - Iliopsoas tendinopathy — Internal snapping hip (coxa saltans interna); snapping sensation at the lesser trochanter - Hip flexor tightness — Anterior pelvic tilt; lumbar hyperlordosis; common in sedentary populations - Thomas test — Assesses hip flexor contracture - Femoral nerve stretch — Psoas spasm can compress the femoral nerve → anterior thigh symptoms

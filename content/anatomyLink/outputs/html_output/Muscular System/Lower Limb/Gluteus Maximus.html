Gluteus Maximus

The gluteus maximus is the largest and most superficial gluteal muscle and the most powerful extensor of the hip. It forms the bulk of the buttock and is critical for climbing, running, and rising from a seated position.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Hip Bone (posterior ilium, posterior gluteal line) + Sacrum (posterior surface) + Coccyx - Insertion: Femur (gluteal tuberosity — deep fibers) + iliotibial band / IT band (superficial fibers → lateral tibial condyle via Gerdy's tubercle)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Posterior Hip Bone + Sacrum + Coccyx
Insertion Gluteal tuberosity (Femur) + iliotibial band
Action Hip extension (primary); hip external rotation; trunk extension; IT band tension
Innervation Inferior gluteal nerve (L5, S1, S2)
Blood Supply Inferior and superior gluteal arteries

🔑 IT Band (Iliotibial Band)

The gluteus maximus's superficial fibers merge with the tensor fasciae latae to form the IT band — a thick fascia running lateral thigh to the lateral knee.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Inferior gluteal nerve injury — Difficulty climbing stairs, rising from a chair; Trendelenburg gait absent (gluteus medius preserved) - Gluteus maximus wasting — L5/S1 nerve root lesion; sedentary lifestyle - IT band syndrome (ITBS) — Lateral knee pain in runners; IT band rubbing over the lateral femoral condyle - Gluteal injection site — Upper outer quadrant of the buttock (avoids sciatic nerve) - Piriformis syndrome — Often confused; sciatic nerve passes below (or through) piriformis, not gluteus maximus

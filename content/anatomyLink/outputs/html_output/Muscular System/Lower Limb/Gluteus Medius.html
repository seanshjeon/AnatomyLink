Gluteus Medius

The gluteus medius is the primary hip abductor and is critical for pelvic stability during walking. Weakness produces the Trendelenburg sign.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Hip Bone (outer ilium — between anterior and posterior gluteal lines) - Insertion: Femur (greater trochanter — lateral and superior surfaces)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Outer ilium (Hip Bone)
Insertion Greater trochanter (Femur)
Action Hip abduction (all fibers); internal rotation (anterior fibers); external rotation (posterior fibers)
Innervation Superior gluteal nerve (L4, L5, S1)
Blood Supply Superior gluteal artery

🔑 Trendelenburg Sign

When standing on one leg, the gluteus medius of the stance leg must contract to prevent the pelvis from dropping on the opposite side. Weakness → Trendelenburg sign (pelvis drops to the contralateral side) and Trendelenburg gait (trunk lurches over the weak side to compensate).

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Trendelenburg gait — Gluteus medius weakness; superior gluteal nerve injury or L5 nerve root lesion - Greater trochanteric pain syndrome (GTPS) — Gluteus medius tendinopathy at the greater trochanter; lateral hip pain - Total hip arthroplasty — Gluteus medius repair is critical; posterior approach risks superior gluteal nerve - Hip abductor strengthening — Key component of lower back and knee rehabilitation programs

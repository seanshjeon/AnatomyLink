Tibialis Anterior

The tibialis anterior is the largest and most medial muscle of the anterior compartment of the leg. It is the primary dorsiflexor and invertor of the foot, and is critical for the swing phase of walking.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Tibia (lateral surface of upper 2/3) + interosseous membrane - Insertion: Tarsals (medial cuneiform — medial and inferior surfaces) + Metatarsals (1st metatarsal base)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Lateral Tibia (upper 2/3) + interosseous membrane
Insertion Medial cuneiform (Tarsals) + 1st metatarsal base (Metatarsals)
Action Foot dorsiflexion (primary); foot inversion
Innervation Common Fibular Nerve Deep fibular (peroneal) nerve (L4, L5)
Blood Supply Anterior tibial artery

🔑 Role in Gait

- Swing phase — Dorsiflexes the foot to clear the ground (prevents foot drop) - Heel strike — Eccentrically controls plantarflexion to lower the forefoot to the ground

🔑 Anterior Compartment Muscles

Tibialis anterior runs with: - Extensor hallucis longus - Extensor digitorum longus - Fibularis (peroneus) tertius

All supplied by the deep fibular nerve and anterior tibial artery.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Foot drop (Steppage gait) — Deep fibular nerve or common fibular nerve injury → inability to dorsiflex → foot slaps the ground - Tibialis anterior tendinopathy — Overuse in runners; pain along anterior ankle - Anterior compartment syndrome — Increased pressure in the anterior compartment compresses tibialis anterior and the deep fibular nerve → foot drop + intense anterior leg pain - L4 nerve root lesion — Tibialis anterior weakness is the key clinical finding; tested by asking patient to walk on heels

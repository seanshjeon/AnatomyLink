Quadriceps Femoris

The quadriceps femoris is a group of 4 muscles forming the anterior thigh — the sole extensor of the knee and one of the most powerful muscle groups in the body.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: All 4 originate from the Femur or Hip Bone - Insertion: All 4 converge into the quadriceps tendon → Patella → patellar tendon → Tibia (tibial tuberosity)

🗂️ The Four Muscles

Muscle Origin Special Feature
Rectus femoris AIIS (Hip Bone) Only head crossing the hip joint
Vastus lateralis Lateral Femur (linea aspera + greater trochanter) Largest of the four
Vastus medialis Medial Femur (linea aspera) VMO stabilizes the patella medially
Vastus intermedius Anterior Femur shaft Deep; beneath rectus femoris

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Insertion Tibial tuberosity (Tibia) via quadriceps tendon → Patella → patellar tendon
Action Knee extension (all heads); hip flexion (rectus femoris only)
Innervation Femoral Nerve (L2–L4) — all four heads
Blood Supply Femoral artery (muscular branches)

🔑 Vastus Medialis Oblique (VMO)

The distal fibers of vastus medialis run obliquely (VMO) and are the primary medial stabilizer of the patella. VMO weakness → lateral patellar tracking → patellofemoral pain syndrome.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Quadriceps tendon rupture — Usually in patients >40 years; inability to extend knee; palpable defect above patella - Patellar tendon rupture — Usually in patients <40 years; Jumper's knee progression - Femoral nerve injury — Loss of knee extension; difficulty walking on stairs - Patellofemoral pain syndrome (PFPS) — VMO weakness + lateral patellar tilt - ACL injury rehabilitation — Quadriceps strengthening is a key component

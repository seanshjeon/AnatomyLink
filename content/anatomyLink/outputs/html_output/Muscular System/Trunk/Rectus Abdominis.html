Rectus Abdominis

The rectus abdominis is the paired longitudinal muscle of the anterior abdominal wall, enclosed within the rectus sheath. It is the primary flexor of the trunk.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Hip Bone (pubic crest + pubic symphysis) - Insertion: Sternum (xiphoid process) + Ribs (costal cartilages 5–7)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Pubic crest & symphysis (Hip Bone)
Insertion Xiphoid process (Sternum) + costal cartilages 5–7 (Ribs)
Action Trunk flexion; posterior pelvic tilt; increases intra-abdominal pressure; assists forced expiration
Innervation Intercostal nerves T7–T12 (anterior rami)
Blood Supply Superior & inferior epigastric arteries

🔑 Rectus Sheath

Formed by aponeuroses of external oblique, internal oblique, and transversus abdominis: - Above arcuate line: anterior + posterior sheath present - Below arcuate line: all aponeuroses pass anteriorly → posterior sheath absent (only transversalis fascia)

🔑 Tendinous Intersections

- 3–4 horizontal bands create the visible "six-pack" appearance - Fused to the anterior rectus sheath only

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Rectus sheath hematoma — Inferior epigastric artery injury (coughing, anticoagulation) - Diastasis recti — Midline separation of the two recti (pregnancy, obesity) - Rectus abdominis myocutaneous (RAM) flap — Common reconstructive option in plastic surgery - Core stability — Works with transversus abdominis and multifidus for spinal stability

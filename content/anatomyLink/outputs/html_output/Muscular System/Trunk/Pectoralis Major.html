Pectoralis Major

The pectoralis major is the large, fan-shaped muscle of the anterior chest wall, forming the anterior axillary fold. It powerfully moves the shoulder in flexion, adduction, and internal rotation.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Clavicle (medial 1/2 — clavicular head) + Sternum (sternal head) + Ribs (costal cartilages 1–6) - Insertion: Humerus (lateral lip of intertubercular groove)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Medial Clavicle + Sternum + costal cartilages 1–6 (Ribs)
Insertion Lateral lip of bicipital groove (Humerus)
Action Clavicular head: shoulder flexion; Sternal head: extension from flexed position; Both: adduction + internal rotation
Innervation Medial & lateral pectoral nerves (C5–T1)
Blood Supply Pectoral branch of thoracoacromial artery; anterior intercostal arteries

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Pectoralis major tear — Bench press injury; "popping" sensation + sudden weakness + ecchymosis - Pectoralis major flap — Reconstructive surgery for head/neck defects and sternal wounds - Breast anatomy — Breast tissue lies anterior to pec major; relevant in mastectomy - Poland syndrome — Congenital absence of the sternal head of pectoralis major

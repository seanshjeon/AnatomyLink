Triceps Brachii

The triceps brachii is the only muscle of the posterior arm and the sole extensor of the elbow. It has three heads (long, lateral, and medial).

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: - Long head: Infraglenoid tubercle of Scapula - Lateral head: Posterior Humerus (above radial groove) - Medial head: Posterior Humerus (below radial groove — deepest head) - Insertion: Ulna (olecranon)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Infraglenoid tubercle (Scapula) + posterior Humerus (2 heads)
Insertion Olecranon of Ulna
Action Elbow extension (all heads); shoulder extension (long head)
Innervation Radial Nerve (C6–C8) — all three heads
Blood Supply Deep brachial artery (profunda brachii)

🔑 Three Heads

- Long head — Only head crossing the shoulder joint; assists in shoulder extension and adduction - Lateral head — Primary elbow extensor in powerful extension - Medial head — "Workhorse" head; active in all elbow extension

🔑 Radial Nerve Relationship

The radial nerve winds in the spiral (radial) groove of the humerus, between the medial and lateral heads of triceps — a critical anatomical relationship for clinical injury assessment.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Radial nerve palsy — Humeral shaft fracture or Saturday night palsy (nerve compression in spiral groove) → wrist drop; triceps is usually spared as the nerve injury is often distal to its branches - Olecranon bursitis — Bursa over olecranon; swollen, tender elbow prominence - Triceps tendon rupture — Rare; fall on outstretched hand + eccentric contraction; unable to extend elbow - Triceps testing — Tests radial nerve and C7 nerve root (reflex: triceps jerk)

Deltoid

The deltoid is the large, triangular muscle forming the rounded contour of the shoulder. It is the prime mover of shoulder abduction and has three distinct functional portions.

🔗 Bone Attachments

- Origin: Clavicle (lateral 1/3 — anterior part) + Scapula (acromion — middle part + spine of scapula — posterior part) - Insertion: Humerus (deltoid tuberosity, mid-lateral shaft)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Origin Lateral Clavicle + acromion + scapular spine (Scapula)
Insertion Deltoid tuberosity (Humerus)
Innervation Axillary Nerve (C5, C6)
Blood Supply Posterior circumflex humeral artery + deltoid branch of thoracoacromial artery

🔑 Three Portions

Part Fibers Primary Action
Anterior (clavicular) Flexion fibers Shoulder flexion + internal rotation
Middle (acromial) Abduction fibers Shoulder abduction (15°–90°)
Posterior (spinal) Extension fibers Shoulder extension + external rotation

Note: The first 15° of abduction is initiated by the supraspinatus (Rotator Cuff); deltoid takes over from 15°–90°. Above 90° requires scapular upward rotation (Trapezius + serratus anterior).

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Axillary nerve injury — Surgical neck fracture of Humerus or anterior shoulder dislocation - Result: deltoid paralysis → flat shoulder contour ("shoulder pad sign"), loss of abduction, loss of sensation over deltoid (regimental badge area) - Deltoid injection site — IM injection in the middle deltoid (lateral arm, 2–3 fingers below acromion) - Deltoid wasting — Axillary nerve injury or C5 root lesion - Anterior deltoid overuse — Common in bench press, overhead pressing

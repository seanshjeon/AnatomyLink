Subcortical Fiber Tracts

Subcortical fiber tracts are the white matter pathways connecting cortical and subcortical regions. The most important are the internal capsule (motor/sensory highway) and the corpus callosum (interhemispheric connection).

🔗 Key Connections

- Brain — cortex connects to subcortical structures via these tracts - Spinal Cord — corticospinal tract descends through internal capsule → brainstem → cord - Basal Ganglia — surround the internal capsule - Brainstem — all ascending and descending tracts pass through

🗂️ Major Tract Systems

1. Projection Fibers — Cortex ↔ Subcortical

Connect cortex to deep structures; most pass through the internal capsule.

#### Internal Capsule A compact white matter structure between the Basal Ganglia (caudate/putamen medially, globus pallidus laterally) and thalamus.

` Anterior limb Genu Posterior limb [A] [G] [P] ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ Frontopont. | Corticob. | Corticospinal │ │ fibers | fibers | (somatotopic) │ └──────────────────────────────────────────┘ Caudate ←→ Thalamus `

Part Contents Function
Anterior limb Frontopontine fibers; anterior thalamic radiation Prefrontal ↔ thalamus; frontal ↔ pons
Genu Corticobulbar fibers Motor commands to cranial nerve nuclei
Posterior limb Corticospinal tract (leg anterior, arm posterior); sensory radiation Voluntary limb movement; touch/pain to cortex
Retrolenticular Optic radiation (LGN → V1); auditory radiation Visual + auditory pathways
Sublenticular Temporopontine fibers Temporal lobe → pons

Somatotopy in posterior limb (anterior → posterior): Face → Arm → Leg

#### Corticospinal Tract (Pyramidal Tract) ` Motor cortex (precentral gyrus) → Corona radiata → Posterior limb internal capsule → Cerebral peduncle (midbrain) → Pyramids (medulla) → Pyramidal decussation (85–90% cross) → Lateral corticospinal tract → Anterior horn cells (LMN) → Muscles ` - UMN signs (above decussation): spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski, weakness - LMN signs (below): flaccidity, hyporeflexia, atrophy, fasciculations

2. Commissural Fibers — Hemisphere ↔ Hemisphere

#### Corpus Callosum The largest white matter commissure; connects homologous areas of both hemispheres.

Part Location Connects
Rostrum Anterior-inferior Prefrontal cortex
Genu Anterior curve Prefrontal cortex (forceps minor)
Body Middle Motor + sensory cortex
Splenium Posterior Occipital + parietal + temporal cortex (forceps major)

Callosal lesions: - Anterior (genu) — Alien hand syndrome (left hand acts independently) - Splenium — Alexia without agraphia (can write but can't read — visual info can't cross) - Complete section — Split-brain syndrome: each hemisphere unaware of the other

#### Anterior Commissure Connects bilateral amygdalae and anterior temporal lobes; smaller than corpus callosum.

3. Association Fibers — Cortex ↔ Cortex (Same Hemisphere)

Connect different cortical areas within the same hemisphere.

Tract Connects Function
Arcuate fasciculus Broca's ↔ Wernicke's area Language (damage → conduction aphasia)
Uncinate fasciculus Frontal ↔ temporal lobe Memory, emotional behavior
Cingulum Cingulate gyrus tract Limbic system connections
Superior longitudinal fasciculus Frontal ↔ parietal/occipital Spatial attention
Inferior longitudinal fasciculus Occipital ↔ temporal Visual object recognition

🏥 Clinical Significance

Condition Lesion Features
Internal capsule stroke Lenticulostriate arteries (MCA branches) Contralateral pure motor hemiplegia ± sensory loss; most common cause of lacunar stroke
Conduction aphasia Arcuate fasciculus Fluent speech + comprehension intact; unable to repeat
Callosal disconnection Corpus callosum Split-brain phenomena; alien hand syndrome
MS plaques Periventricular white matter Variable deficits; classic dawson's fingers

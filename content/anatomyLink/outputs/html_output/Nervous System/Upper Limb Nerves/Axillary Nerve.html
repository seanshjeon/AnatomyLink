Axillary Nerve

The axillary nerve (C5, C6) is a terminal branch of the posterior cord of the Brachial Plexus that innervates the deltoid and teres minor and provides sensation to the regimental badge area of the lateral upper arm.

🔗 Origin

- Root values: C5–C6 - From plexus: Brachial Plexus - Cord: Posterior cord

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Root Values C5–C6
Plexus Origin Brachial Plexus (posterior cord)
Course Exits axilla through the quadrangular space with the posterior circumflex humeral artery; winds around the surgical neck of the Humerus
Sensory Territory Regimental badge area — lateral upper arm (over lower deltoid)
Terminal Branches Anterior branch (motor to deltoid), posterior branch (motor to teres minor + superior lateral cutaneous nerve of arm)

💪 Muscles Innervated

- Deltoid (anterior, middle, and posterior parts) — shoulder abduction (middle), flexion (anterior), extension (posterior) - Teres Minor (part of Rotator Cuff) — lateral rotation of the arm

🦴 Key Anatomical Relationships

- Passes through the quadrangular space, bounded by: - Superiorly: teres minor / subscapularis - Inferiorly: teres major - Medially: long head of Triceps Brachii - Laterally: surgical neck of Humerus - Travels with the posterior circumflex humeral artery - Lies in close contact with the surgical neck of the Humerus — the most clinically important relationship

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Axillary nerve palsy — caused by surgical neck fracture of the Humerus or anterior shoulder dislocation - Motor deficit: deltoid paralysis → flat (squared-off) shoulder, loss of shoulder abduction (0–15° arc), weak flexion and extension - Sensory deficit: loss of sensation over the regimental badge area (lateral upper arm) - Test: inability to initiate abduction against resistance - Anterior shoulder dislocation — humeral head displaces anteriorly and inferiorly, stretching the axillary nerve - Quadrangular space syndrome — rare compression causing posterior shoulder pain and deltoid weakness - Distinguish from suprascapular nerve injury (supraspinatus + infraspinatus weakness, different sensory territory)

Sacral Plexus

The sacral plexus is formed by the lumbosacral trunk (L4–L5) + anterior rami of S1–S3 on the anterior surface of piriformis, deep in the pelvis. It supplies the posterior thigh, entire leg and foot, gluteal muscles, and perineum.

🔗 Key Connections

- Root values: L4–S3 (via lumbosacral trunk from Lumbar Plexus) - Formed on: anterior surface of piriformis muscle, against Sacrum and Hip Bone - Exits via: greater sciatic foramen (most branches) - Key terminal nerve: Sciatic Nerve (largest nerve in the body) - Relation to: Iliac Arteries (internal iliac vessels cross anterior to plexus)

🗂️ Branches

Nerve Roots Exit Supply
Sciatic Nerve L4–S3 Greater sciatic foramen (below piriformis) Entire lower limb below knee via Tibial Nerve + Common Fibular Nerve; posterior thigh muscles (Hamstrings)
Superior gluteal nerve L4–S1 Greater sciatic foramen (above piriformis) Gluteus Medius, gluteus minimus, tensor fasciae latae
Inferior gluteal nerve L5–S2 Greater sciatic foramen (below piriformis) Gluteus Maximus
Nerve to piriformis S1–S2 Direct Piriformis
Nerve to obturator internus L5–S2 Lesser sciatic foramen Obturator internus, superior gemellus
Nerve to quadratus femoris L4–S1 Below piriformis Quadratus femoris, inferior gemellus
Posterior cutaneous nerve of thigh S1–S3 Below piriformis Skin of posterior thigh, perineum, posterior scrotum/labia
Pudendal nerve S2–S4 Lesser sciatic foramen (via Alcock's canal) External anal sphincter, external urethral sphincter, perineal muscles, genitalia; somatic sensation of perineum
Pelvic splanchnics S2–S4 Direct into pelvis Parasympathetic to pelvic viscera (bladder, rectum, uterus — "erection and erection" S2–S4)

🔑 The Piriformis Divide

The piriformis muscle is the key landmark — almost all sacral plexus branches exit the greater sciatic foramen either above (superior gluteal) or below (everything else) piriformis.

🔑 Parasympathetic Output (S2–S4)

The pelvic splanchnic nerves carry the sacral parasympathetic outflow — responsible for: - Bladder detrusor contraction (micturition) - Rectal motility + defecation - Penile/clitoral erection ("point, shoot, score" S2–S4 erection; T10–L2 emission; S2–S4 ejaculation)

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Sciatic nerve injury — see Sciatic Nerve; posterior hip dislocation most common cause at this level - Piriformis syndrome — sciatic nerve irritation by piriformis (especially if nerve passes through muscle); buttock pain + sciatica; positive FAIR test - Superior gluteal nerve injury — pelvic surgery or posterior approach hip arthroplasty; Gluteus Medius paralysis → Trendelenburg gait (pelvis drops on opposite side when weight-bearing) - Pudendal nerve injury — childbirth (stretching over ischial spine); faecal + urinary incontinence; sexual dysfunction; perineal anaesthesia - Cauda equina syndrome — technically involves lumbar + sacral roots before they join the plexus; see Spinal Cord - Sacral insufficiency fracture — elderly + osteoporotic; H-pattern fracture on bone scan; can compress sacral plexus roots - Pudendal nerve block — used in obstetric analgesia; needle directed toward ischial spine (pudendal nerve wraps around it)

Lumbar Plexus

The lumbar plexus is formed by the anterior rami of L1–L4 (with a contribution from T12) within the substance of Iliopsoas, supplying the anterior and medial compartments of the thigh and the inguinal region.

🔗 Key Connections

- Root values: L1–L4 (+ T12 contribution) - Formed within: Iliopsoas (psoas major muscle) - Relation to: Lumbar Vertebrae (roots exit intervertebral foramina) - Joins with: Sacral Plexus (via lumbosacral trunk L4–L5) → lumbosacral plexus - Key terminal nerve: Femoral Nerve

🗂️ Branches

Nerve Roots Course Supply
Iliohypogastric L1 (T12) Above inguinal ligament Skin of hypogastrium + lateral buttock
Ilioinguinal L1 Inguinal canal Skin of medial thigh, scrotum/labia majora, mons pubis
Genitofemoral L1–L2 On anterior surface of Iliopsoas → inguinal canal Genital branch: cremaster reflex; femoral branch: skin of femoral triangle
Lateral femoral cutaneous (LFCN) L2–L3 Exits beneath inguinal ligament (medial to ASIS) Lateral thigh sensation only (purely sensory)
Obturator nerve L2–L4 Through obturator foramen (Hip Bone) Adductor muscles; medial thigh sensation
Femoral Nerve L2–L4 Exits below inguinal ligament (lateral to femoral artery) Quadriceps Femoris, Iliopsoas, sartorius, pectineus; anterior thigh + medial leg (saphenous)
Lumbosacral trunk L4–L5 Crosses pelvic brim → joins Sacral Plexus Contribution to sciatic + superior/inferior gluteal nerves

🔑 Femoral Triangle and the Lumbar Plexus

In the femoral triangle, branches emerge lateral to medial: Femoral Nerve → femoral artery (Femoral Artery) → femoral vein — mnemonic NAVY (Nerve, Artery, Vein, Y-fronts/lymphatics) lateral to medial.

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Meralgia paraesthetica — entrapment of lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (LFCN) at inguinal ligament → burning/tingling/numbness of lateral thigh; risk: obesity, tight belts, pregnancy; purely sensory (no motor loss) - Femoral nerve palsy — see Femoral Nerve; most commonly from inguinal ligament compression, haematoma, pelvic mass, or during hip surgery - Obturator nerve injury — pelvic surgery, obturator hernia, childbirth → adductor weakness + medial thigh numbness; difficulty crossing legs - Ilioinguinal nerve entrapment — post-hernia repair pain (groin + inner thigh + scrotum/labia); common complication of open inguinal herniorrhaphy - Psoas abscess — infection within Iliopsoas can compress lumbar plexus → leg pain + hip flexion deformity; caused by TB of Lumbar Vertebrae (Pott's disease) or Crohn's disease

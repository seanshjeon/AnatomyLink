Spinal Cord

The spinal cord is the cylindrical CNS structure extending from the foramen magnum to L1–L2 (conus medullaris), transmitting sensory and motor signals between the brain and body via 31 pairs of spinal nerves.

🔗 Key Connections

- Nervous System — parent system - Brain — superior continuation - Vertebral Column — bony canal protection - Cervical Vertebrae — C1–C7 (cervical cord segments C1–C8) - Thoracic Vertebrae — T1–T12 (thoracic cord segments) - Lumbar Vertebrae — L1–L2 vertebral level (conus medullaris); lumbar cord segments pass through - Sacrum — sacral segments S1–S5 pass through sacral canal - Brachial Plexus — arises from C5–T1 nerve roots - Lumbar Plexus — arises from L1–L4 nerve roots - Sacral Plexus — arises from L4–S3 nerve roots

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Length ~45 cm in adults
Extent Foramen magnum → conus medullaris at L1–L2
Below conus Cauda equina ("horse's tail") — nerve roots descend to exit at their vertebral levels
Segments 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal = 31 pairs
Enlargements Cervical (C5–T1, upper limb) and lumbosacral (L1–S3, lower limb)
Filum terminale Fibrous extension of pia anchoring cord to coccyx

🔑 Gray Matter — H-Shaped Cross Section

Horn Type Function
Dorsal (posterior) horn Sensory Receives incoming sensory signals from dorsal root ganglia
Ventral (anterior) horn Motor Lower motor neurons (LMNs) → efferent axons exit via ventral roots
Lateral horn Autonomic Present T1–L2 (sympathetic preganglionic) and S2–S4 (parasympathetic)

Dorsal root = sensory (afferent); Ventral root = motor (efferent). They unite to form a mixed spinal nerve (Bell-Magendie Law).

🔑 Major White Matter Tracts

Tract Column Direction Function
Lateral corticospinal tract Lateral funiculus Descending (motor) Voluntary limb movement; decussates in medullary pyramids
Anterior corticospinal tract Anterior funiculus Descending (motor) Axial/trunk voluntary movement; decussates at segmental level
Dorsal columns (fasciculus gracilis & cuneatus) Posterior funiculus Ascending (sensory) Fine touch, vibration, proprioception; decussates in medulla
Spinothalamic tract (anterolateral) Anterior/lateral funiculus Ascending (sensory) Pain and temperature; decussates within 1–2 levels of entry
Spinocerebellar tracts Lateral funiculus Ascending (sensory) Unconscious proprioception to cerebellum
Rubrospinal tract Lateral funiculus Descending (motor) Limb flexion (minor in humans)

🏥 Clinical Significance

Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) Levels

Level Motor Deficit Sensory Loss
C4 Diaphragm paralysis (phrenic nerve C3-5) Below neck
C5–C6 Shoulder abduction/elbow flexion Lateral arm/forearm
C7 Triceps, wrist extensors Middle finger
T4 Below nipple line Below nipple (T4)
T10 Below umbilicus Below umbilicus (T10)
L4 Knee extension (quadriceps), ankle dorsiflexion Medial leg
S1 Ankle plantarflexion Lateral foot, sole
S2–S4 Bladder, bowel, sexual function Perineum (saddle area)

Brown-Séquard Syndrome (Hemicord Lesion)

Caused by lateral cord hemisection (e.g., stabbing): - Ipsilateral — UMN signs (spastic paresis), loss of proprioception/vibration (dorsal column) - Contralateral — loss of pain and temperature (spinothalamic, decussates within cord 1–2 levels)

Cauda Equina Syndrome

Compression of nerve roots below L1–L2 (not true spinal cord — LMN lesion): - Signs — saddle anesthesia (S2–S5), bilateral leg weakness, bowel/bladder incontinence - Cause — large central disc herniation, tumor, hematoma - Surgical emergency — irreversible if not decompressed promptly

Other Syndromes

Syndrome Lesion Key Features
Central cord syndrome Central gray (most common SCI) Arms weaker than legs, urinary retention
Anterior cord syndrome Anterior spinal artery occlusion Bilateral motor loss + pain/temp loss; proprioception spared
Posterior cord syndrome Dorsal columns Loss of proprioception/vibration; motor and pain/temp intact
Syringomyelia Central canal expansion Cape-like bilateral loss of pain/temp (hands), UMN signs in legs
ALS UMN + LMN Combined spastic paresis (UMN) + fasciculations/atrophy (LMN)

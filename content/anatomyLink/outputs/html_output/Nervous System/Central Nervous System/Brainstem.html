Brainstem

The brainstem connects the Brain to the Spinal Cord and consists of three parts: midbrain (mesencephalon), pons, and medulla oblongata. It houses cranial nerve nuclei III–XII, controls vital functions, and contains all ascending and descending pathways.

🔗 Key Connections

- Brain — superior continuation (diencephalon above) - Spinal Cord — inferior continuation through foramen magnum - Cranial Nerves — CN III–XII nuclei housed within brainstem - Cerebellum — connected via three cerebellar peduncles

🗂️ Three Divisions

1. Midbrain (Mesencephalon)

Structure Function
Cerebral peduncles Corticospinal + corticobulbar tracts descend
Tectum (superior/inferior colliculi) Superior: visual reflexes; Inferior: auditory reflexes
Tegmentum CN III, IV nuclei; red nucleus; substantia nigra
Substantia nigra Dopamine-producing; connects to Basal Ganglia
Red nucleus Motor coordination; rubrospinal tract
Cerebral aqueduct CSF channel connecting 3rd → 4th ventricle

Cranial nerves: CN III (oculomotor), CN IV (trochlear)

2. Pons

Structure Function
Basis pontis Corticospinal/corticopontine tracts; pontine nuclei
Tegmentum CN V–VIII nuclei; reticular formation
Middle cerebellar peduncles Largest peduncle; cortex → cerebellum input
Pneumotaxic + apneustic centers Respiratory rhythm modulation
Locus coeruleus Major noradrenergic nucleus; arousal

Cranial nerves: CN V (trigeminal), CN VI (abducens), CN VII (facial), CN VIII (vestibulocochlear)

3. Medulla Oblongata

Structure Function
Pyramids Corticospinal tract; pyramidal decussation (85–90% cross here)
Olive (inferior olivary nucleus) Relay to cerebellum; motor learning
Reticular formation Vital centers: cardiac, vasomotor, respiratory
Nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) Visceral afferents; taste; autonomic reflex
Dorsal motor nucleus of vagus Parasympathetic preganglionic (CN X)
Hypoglossal nucleus CN XII — tongue movement

Cranial nerves: CN IX (glossopharyngeal), CN X (vagus), CN XI (accessory), CN XII (hypoglossal)

🔑 Cerebellar Peduncles

Peduncle Location Main Connections Direction
Superior (SCP) Midbrain Cerebellum → thalamus (dentothalamic) Output
Middle (MCP) Pons Pons → cerebellum (pontocerebellar) Input
Inferior (ICP) Medulla Spinal cord + medulla → cerebellum Input

🔑 Reticular Formation

Diffuse network throughout the brainstem tegmentum: - ARAS (Ascending Reticular Activating System) — Arousal and consciousness; projects to thalamus → cortex - Vital centers — Cardiac, vasomotor, respiratory control (medulla) - Sleep-wake regulation — Interacts with hypothalamus

🏥 Clinical Significance

Brainstem Syndromes (Crossed Findings)

Syndrome Lesion Site Key Features
Weber syndrome Midbrain (CN III + cerebral peduncle) Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hemiplegia
Benedikt syndrome Midbrain tegmentum Ipsilateral CN III + contralateral tremor/ataxia (red nucleus)
Millard-Gubler Pons (CN VI, VII + CST) Ipsilateral CN VI + VII + contralateral hemiplegia
Wallenberg (PICA) Lateral medulla Ipsilateral: face pain/temp loss, Horner, ataxia, dysphagia; Contralateral: body pain/temp loss
Locked-in syndrome Ventral pons (bilateral) Quadriplegia + anarthria; consciousness preserved; vertical eye movement + blinking intact

Herniation Syndromes

- Transtentorial (uncal) — Midbrain compression → ipsilateral CN III palsy ("blown pupil") - Tonsillar — Cerebellar tonsils compress medulla → respiratory arrest

Brain Death

- Absent brainstem reflexes: pupillary, corneal, oculocephalic, gag, cough - Apnea test confirms loss of medullary respiratory drive

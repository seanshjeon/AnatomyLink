Femoral Nerve

The femoral nerve (L2–L4), the largest branch of the Lumbar Plexus, innervates the quadriceps femoris and hip flexors and provides sensation to the anterior thigh and medial leg via its saphenous branch — the longest cutaneous nerve in the body.

🔗 Origin

- Root values: L2–L4 - From plexus: Lumbar Plexus - Cord: N/A (lumbar plexus — no cord equivalent)

🗂️ Key Information

Property Detail
Root Values L2–L4
Plexus Origin Lumbar Plexus
Course Forms within Iliopsoas (psoas major) → emerges from the lateral border of psoas → passes under the inguinal ligament (lateral to femoral artery — mnemonic NAVY: nerve, artery, vein, Y-fronts) → enters femoral triangle → divides into anterior and posterior divisions; saphenous nerve descends medially to supply medial leg + foot
Sensory Territory Anterior and medial thigh (intermediate + medial cutaneous nerves of thigh); medial leg + medial foot (saphenous nerve — longest cutaneous nerve in body)
Terminal Branches Anterior division (medial + intermediate cutaneous nerves of thigh; motor to sartorius); Posterior division (motor to quadriceps; saphenous nerve)

💪 Muscles Innervated

- Iliopsoas (iliacus component innervated in the abdomen, before inguinal ligament) — hip flexion (primary) - Quadriceps Femoris (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius) — knee extension (primary); rectus femoris also flexes hip - Sartorius — hip flexion + external rotation + abduction; knee flexion (FABER position) - Pectineus — hip adduction + flexion (also receives obturator nerve contribution)

🦴 Key Anatomical Relationships

- Femoral nerve is lateral to the femoral artery under the inguinal ligament (contained in the muscular lacuna, not the femoral sheath) - Femoral triangle boundaries: inguinal ligament (superior), sartorius (lateral), adductor longus (medial), iliopsoas + pectineus (floor) - Saphenous nerve accompanies the femoral artery into the adductor canal (Hunter's canal), then exits through the subsartorial fascia, descends medial to the knee with the great saphenous vein, continuing to the medial malleolus and medial foot - Iliacus is innervated proximal to the inguinal ligament — partially spared in distal injuries

🏥 Clinical Significance

- Femoral nerve palsy — loss of knee extension is the hallmark deficit - Motor deficit: quadriceps paralysis → cannot extend knee (cannot climb stairs, difficulty walking); weak hip flexion - Reflex: absent knee jerk (L3–L4) - Sensory deficit: anterior and medial thigh; medial leg (via saphenous nerve) - Gait: quadriceps-avoidance gait (locks knee in extension, leans forward over the knee) - Causes of femoral nerve injury: - Inguinal ligament compression (lithotomy position, prolonged hip flexion) - Pelvic haematoma (haemophilia, anticoagulation) — compresses nerve in iliacus fascia - Pelvic mass / psoas abscess - Diabetic amyotrophy (lumbosacral plexopathy affecting L2–L4) - Total hip replacement / hip surgery - Femoral artery catheterisation - Femoral nerve stretch test — patient prone, knee flexed; hip extended → stretches L2–L4 roots; positive for femoral nerve or high lumbar root irritation - Saphenous nerve entrapment — medial knee or medial leg pain/paraesthesia; can be confused with medial knee pathology; occurs at the adductor canal - Meralgia paraesthetica (lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, NOT femoral nerve) — anterolateral thigh tingling; note this is a distinct nerve (L2–L3, no motor component)

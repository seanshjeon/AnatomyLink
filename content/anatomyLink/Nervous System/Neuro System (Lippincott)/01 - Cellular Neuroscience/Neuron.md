# 🧠 Neuron

> The neuron is the **fundamental structural and functional unit of the nervous system**, consisting of a **soma, dendrites, and axon**, specialized for **receiving, integrating, and transmitting electrical signals** across synapses to other neurons, muscles, or glands.

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Size | Soma: 5–100 µm diameter; axons up to 1 m long |
| Resting membrane potential | −70 mV |
| Threshold for AP | ~−55 mV |
| Number in human CNS | ~86 billion neurons |
| Post-mitotic status | Largely post-mitotic after development (exceptions: olfactory bulb, hippocampal dentate gyrus) |
| Primary energy substrate | Glucose (aerobic); highly sensitive to hypoxia |

## 🔑 Neuronal Structure

### Soma (Cell Body)
The soma is the metabolic center of the neuron. It contains:

| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| Nucleus (large, pale, prominent nucleolus) | Gene expression, RNA synthesis |
| Rough endoplasmic reticulum (Nissl substance) | Protein synthesis; disperses in injury (chromatolysis) |
| Golgi apparatus | Post-translational modification, vesicle packaging |
| Mitochondria | ATP production via oxidative phosphorylation |
| Cytoskeleton (neurofilaments, microtubules) | Structural support; scaffolding for axonal transport |
| Lysosomes | Degradation of cellular debris |

### Dendrites
- Short, branching processes arising from the soma
- Highly branched (dendritic tree) — greatly increases receptive surface area
- Contain **dendritic spines** (small protrusions) on excitatory synapses, expressing AMPA/NMDA receptors
- Conduct electrotonically (graded potentials), not action potentials (in most cases)
- Contain ribosomes locally — allow local protein synthesis in response to activity

### Axon and Axon Hillock
| Structure | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Axon hillock | Cone-shaped initial segment; highest density of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; site of AP initiation |
| Initial segment | 20–60 µm from hillock; where threshold is reached |
| Axon | Single process; conducts AP away from soma; diameter 0.2–20 µm |
| Axon collaterals | Branches allowing divergence of signal |
| Axon terminal (bouton) | Presynaptic ending; contains synaptic vesicles and active zones |
| Nodes of Ranvier | Gaps (~1 µm) in myelin sheath at regular intervals (~1 mm); site of AP regeneration in saltatory conduction |

### Myelin Sheath
- Produced by **oligodendrocytes** (CNS) or **Schwann cells** (PNS)
- Composed of concentric lipid bilayers wrapping around axon segments (internodes)
- Functions: electrical insulation, increases conduction velocity, metabolic support of axon
- **Internodal length**: proportional to axon diameter; longer internodes = faster conduction

## 🔑 Classification of Neurons

### By Number of Processes (Structural)
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Unipolar | Single process (soma extension) | Some invertebrate neurons |
| Pseudounipolar | One T-shaped process (central + peripheral branch); soma off to side | Dorsal root ganglion (DRG) sensory neurons |
| Bipolar | One dendrite + one axon | Retinal bipolar cells, cochlear neurons, olfactory receptor neurons |
| Multipolar | Multiple dendrites + one axon | Motor neurons, interneurons (most common CNS type) |

### By Function (Physiological)
| Type | Function | Location |
|------|----------|----------|
| Sensory (afferent) | Carries signals from periphery to CNS | DRG (somatic); cranial ganglia (special senses) |
| Motor (efferent) | Carries commands from CNS to effectors | Anterior horn (somatic); autonomic ganglia (visceral) |
| Interneuron (association) | Integrates signals within CNS | Spinal cord, cerebral cortex, cerebellum (~99% of all neurons) |

## 🔑 Axonal Transport

Axonal transport is essential because the soma must supply the axon terminal (which can be >1 m away) with proteins, organelles, and lipids. Two directions exist:

### Anterograde Transport (soma → axon terminal)
| Type | Speed | Cargo | Motor Protein |
|------|-------|-------|---------------|
| Fast anterograde | 200–400 mm/day | Synaptic vesicles, membrane proteins, organelles, mitochondria | **Kinesin** (moves along microtubules toward + end, i.e., away from soma) |
| Slow anterograde (SCa) | 0.2–1 mm/day | Neurofilaments, microtubule subunits | Kinesin (slow) |
| Slow anterograde (SCb) | 2–8 mm/day | Actin, metabolic enzymes, clathrin | Kinesin (slow) |

### Retrograde Transport (axon terminal → soma)
| Type | Speed | Cargo | Motor Protein |
|------|-------|-------|---------------|
| Fast retrograde | 100–200 mm/day | Endosomes, autophagosomes, trophic signals (NGF-TrkA), degraded organelles | **Dynein** (moves along microtubules toward − end, i.e., toward soma) |

> **Clinical note:** Retrograde transport is exploited by neurotropic viruses (herpes simplex, rabies, tetanus toxin) to travel from peripheral nerve terminals to the CNS.

## 🔑 Resting Membrane Potential

| Parameter | Value / Detail |
|-----------|----------------|
| Resting membrane potential (Vm) | −70 mV (inside negative relative to outside) |
| Primary determinant | K⁺ equilibrium potential (~−90 mV); K⁺ leak channels dominate at rest |
| Na⁺ equilibrium potential | ~+60 mV |
| Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase contribution | Pumps 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in per cycle; electrogenic (contributes ~−3 to −5 mV directly) |
| Goldman equation | Accounts for relative permeabilities of Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ |
| Intracellular [K⁺] | ~140 mM |
| Extracellular [K⁺] | ~5 mM |
| Intracellular [Na⁺] | ~15 mM |
| Extracellular [Na⁺] | ~145 mM |

## 🔑 Neuronal Polarity

Neurons exhibit strict structural-functional polarity:
- **Dendrites + soma** = input zone (receive and integrate signals)
- **Axon hillock / initial segment** = trigger zone (generates AP if threshold reached)
- **Axon** = conducting zone (propagates AP)
- **Axon terminal** = output zone (releases neurotransmitter)

This polarity is maintained by:
- Differential distribution of ion channels (e.g., voltage-gated Na⁺ channels concentrated at initial segment)
- Axon initial segment (AIS) barrier: cytoskeletal filter (AnkyrinG, βIV-spectrin) prevents somatodendritic proteins from entering axon

## 🏥 Clinical Significance

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Features |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Wallerian degeneration** | Axon severed distal to soma; axon and myelin distal to lesion degenerate within days–weeks | Begins within 24–48 h; macrophages clear debris; prerequisite for regeneration in PNS |
| **Chromatolysis** | Soma response to axonal injury; Nissl substance disperses, nucleus moves peripherally | Indicates axonal injury / retrograde reaction; neuron attempts regeneration |
| **Dying-back neuropathy** (length-dependent axonopathy) | Distal axon degenerates first, progressing proximally; caused by metabolic failure (diabetic neuropathy, toxic neuropathy) | "Stocking-glove" sensory loss; longest axons affected first |
| **Axonal transport defects** | Mutations in kinesin/dynein or disruption of microtubules | Linked to Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease, ALS, Alzheimer's disease |
| **Neurotropic viral invasion** | Viruses (rabies, HSV) use retrograde dynein transport to reach CNS | Explains ascending nature of rabies encephalitis |

# 😴 Sleep and Wakefulness

> **Sleep is an actively generated, highly regulated brain state orchestrated by the interplay of circadian timing (SCN), homeostatic sleep pressure (adenosine), and a brainstem–hypothalamic flip-flop switch** — with distinct NREM and REM stages serving complementary functions in memory consolidation, metabolic restoration, and emotional processing.

## 🔗 Key Connections
- [Hypothalamus](./anatomyLink/Nervous System/Neuro System (Lippincott)/04 - Brain Regions/Hypothalamus.md) — VLPO (sleep-promoting), lateral hypothalamus (orexin/wake-stabilizing), SCN (circadian master clock)
- [Brainstem](./anatomyLink/Nervous System/Central Nervous System/Brainstem.md) — locus coeruleus (NE), raphe nuclei (5-HT), PPT/LDT (ACh), reticular formation (ARAS)
- [Neurotransmitters](./anatomyLink/Nervous System/Neuro System (Lippincott)/01 - Cellular Neuroscience/Neurotransmitters.md) — histamine, NE, serotonin, acetylcholine, orexin, GABA, adenosine, melatonin
- [Memory Systems](./anatomyLink/Nervous System/Neuro System (Lippincott)/05 - Higher Functions/Memory Systems.md) — SWS for declarative memory consolidation; REM for procedural/emotional memory
- [Neurodegenerative Diseases](./anatomyLink/Nervous System/Neuro System (Lippincott)/06 - Clinical Neurology/Neurodegenerative Diseases.md) — RBD as prodromal synucleinopathy; fatal familial insomnia (prion/thalamus)

## 🗂️ Key Information
| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Sleep cycle duration | ~90 minutes; 4–5 cycles per night |
| REM proportion | Increases toward morning (~25% of total sleep time in adults) |
| SWS proportion | Greatest in first third of night; decreases with age |
| Sleep-promoting area | VLPO (ventrolateral preoptic area), anterior hypothalamus |
| Wake-stabilizing peptide | Orexin/hypocretin (lateral hypothalamus) |
| Circadian master clock | Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), anterior hypothalamus |
| Melatonin | Pineal gland; onset at darkness (DLMO ~2h before sleep onset); suppressed by blue light |
| Adenosine | Homeostatic sleep pressure; accumulates during wakefulness; cleared during sleep; caffeine blocks A1/A2A receptors |

## 🔑 Sleep Stages and EEG Characteristics

| Stage | EEG | Frequency | Key Features | Physiology |
|-------|-----|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Wake (eyes open)** | Beta waves | 13–30 Hz | Active, alert | High sympathetic tone; active muscle EMG |
| **Wake (eyes closed, relaxed)** | Alpha waves | 8–12 Hz | Eyes closed, relaxed but awake | Posterior dominant rhythm; disappears on eye opening |
| **N1 (NREM stage 1)** | Theta waves; vertex sharp waves | 4–7 Hz | Transition to sleep; hypnic jerks; slow eye movements; easily awakened | ↓muscle tone; hypnagogic imagery; loss of alpha |
| **N2 (NREM stage 2)** | Sleep spindles + K-complexes | Spindles: 12–15 Hz; K-complexes: slow biphasic | Largest proportion of sleep (~50%); threshold for arousal ↑ | Sleep spindles = thalamic reticular nucleus bursts; K-complexes = cortical response to stimuli; memory consolidation begins |
| **N3 (SWS / slow-wave sleep)** | Delta waves (slow-wave activity) | 0.5–4 Hz, high amplitude | Most restorative stage; hardest to awaken; night terrors + sleepwalking occur here | Growth hormone release (GH pulse); declarative memory consolidation; glymphatic system active (waste clearance) |
| **REM sleep** | Desynchronized EEG; sawtooth waves | Mixed, low-amplitude; sawtooth: 2–5 Hz | Vivid dreams; rapid eye movements; **muscle atonia** (except diaphragm + EOMs); penile/clitoral tumescence | Activated amygdala; emotional/procedural memory consolidation; brain temperature highest; ↑ACh, ↓NE/5-HT |

### Sleep Architecture Across the Night
- **First half**: SWS-rich (NREM 3 dominant) → slow-wave sleep for declarative memory, tissue repair
- **Second half**: REM-rich cycles → emotional processing, procedural memory, dream consolidation
- Waking: N1 → N2 → N3 → N2 → REM (first cycle ~90 min, then repeat with decreasing SWS/increasing REM)

## 🔑 Ascending Reticular Activating System (ARAS) — Wake-Promoting Systems

| System | Key Nuclei | Neurotransmitter | Projections | Wake vs. Sleep Activity |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|------------|------------------------|
| **Histaminergic** | Tuberomammillary nucleus (TMN), posterior hypothalamus | Histamine (H1/H2) | Entire cortex; basal forebrain; thalamus | **Active only during wakefulness**; silent in sleep; histamine H1 antagonists (diphenhydramine) cause sedation |
| **Noradrenergic** | Locus coeruleus (LC), pontine tegmentum | Norepinephrine (NE) | Widespread: cortex, hippocampus, spinal cord | Active in wake, reduced in NREM, **virtually silent in REM** ("REM-off"); stress activates LC |
| **Serotonergic** | Raphe nuclei (dorsal raphe), midbrain | Serotonin (5-HT) | Widespread: cortex, limbic, basal ganglia | Active in wake, ↓in NREM, **silent in REM** ("REM-off"); SSRIs suppress REM |
| **Cholinergic** | Basal forebrain (Ch1–4: medial septum, nbM); PPT/LDT (brainstem) | Acetylcholine (ACh) | Cortex (basal forebrain); thalamus/brainstem (PPT/LDT) | Active in **BOTH wake AND REM** ("REM-on"); cortical desynchronization/EEG activation; muscarinic M1 receptors |
| **Dopaminergic** | VTA, substantia nigra compacta; dorsal raphe DA neurons | Dopamine (DA) | Prefrontal cortex; limbic (mesolimbic); striatum | Wake and arousal; less clear sleep-specific role; amphetamines promote wakefulness via DA |
| **Orexinergic** | Lateral hypothalamus (orexin/hypocretin neurons, ~50,000–80,000 cells) | Orexin-A and Orexin-B (hypocretin 1 and 2) | All arousal nuclei (LC, raphe, TMN, basal forebrain, PPT/LDT, cortex) | **Stabilizes wakefulness**; active during active waking, especially with motivated behaviors; absent in narcolepsy |
| **Glutamatergic ARAS** | Parabrachial nucleus; reticular formation | Glutamate | Basal forebrain; thalamus | Broad arousal; essential for consciousness |

## 🔑 Sleep-Promoting Systems

### VLPO (Ventrolateral Preoptic Area)
- Located in **anterior hypothalamus**, just above the optic chiasm
- Neurons: **GABAergic + galaninergic** (inhibitory)
- During sleep: VLPO neurons fire → release GABA + galanin → **inhibit all arousal nuclei** (LC, raphe, TMN, PPT/LDT)
- During wake: arousal nuclei fire → NE + histamine + 5-HT → **inhibit VLPO** (mutual inhibition)
- VLPO lesions → insomnia (dose-dependent: larger lesion = more insomnia)

### Parafacial Zone (Medulla)
- Ventromedial medulla; GABAergic neurons
- Promotes N2/SWS by inhibiting parabrachial wake-promoting neurons

### Adenosine: Homeostatic Sleep Pressure
- Byproduct of neuronal metabolism; **accumulates during wakefulness** (especially in basal forebrain)
- Acts on A1 receptors (inhibits basal forebrain cholinergic/wake neurons) + A2A receptors (activates VLPO)
- Caffeine: nonselective adenosine receptor antagonist → blocks A1/A2A → ↑wakefulness
- After sleep: adenosine is cleared (restored sensitivity)
- "Sleep debt" = accumulated adenosine drive for sleep

## 🔑 Flip-Flop Switch Model (Saper et al.)

```
     AROUSAL NUCLEI                         VLPO
  (LC + Raphe + TMN + PPT)    ←←←GABA/galanin←←←  [Sleep-ON]
         [Wake-ON]             →→→NE/5-HT/histamine→→→
         
              ↑ OREXIN stabilizes the WAKE side
```

- **Mutual inhibition**: when arousal nuclei are active, they inhibit VLPO; when VLPO is active, it inhibits arousal nuclei
- This creates a **bistable switch** — avoids intermediate states; switches rapidly between wake and sleep
- **Orexin (hypocretin)** input to arousal nuclei stabilizes the wake state; without orexin, the switch becomes unstable
- **Narcolepsy**: loss of orexin neurons → unstable switch → sudden inappropriate transitions from wake to sleep (or REM intrusion)

## 🔑 Circadian Rhythm and the Molecular Clock

### Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN)
- **Master circadian clock** in the anterior hypothalamus (above optic chiasm)
- Self-sustaining ~24h oscillation (slightly longer: ~24.2h → entrained by light)
- Output: regulates melatonin release (via paraventricular nucleus → intermediolateral column → superior cervical ganglion → pineal gland)
- Projects to VLPO and arousal nuclei to time sleep-wake cycles

### Molecular Clock Mechanism
```
CLOCK + BMAL1 (heterodimer) → transcription of Per1/2/3, Cry1/2, Rev-erbα
         ↓
Per + Cry proteins accumulate (8–12h delay) → form PER:CRY complex
         ↓
PER:CRY translocates to nucleus → INHIBITS CLOCK:BMAL1 transcription (negative feedback)
         ↓
Per/Cry mRNA levels fall → PER:CRY complex degraded → CLOCK:BMAL1 activity restored
         ↓
~24-hour cycle repeats
```
- CK1ε/δ phosphorylates PER → ubiquitination → proteasomal degradation (sets period length)
- Mutations: familial advanced sleep phase syndrome (FASPS) = CK1δ or PER2 mutation → ~20h period → very early bedtime/wake

### Light Entrainment
- **Retinohypothalamic tract (RHT)**: retinal ganglion cells (melanopsin-containing ipRGCs, intrinsically photosensitive) → SCN → phase shift
- Morning light → phase advance; evening light → phase delay
- **Melatonin** (pineal): onset at darkness → promotes sleep onset; **DLMO** (dim-light melatonin onset) ~2h before habitual sleep time
- Blue light (peak ~480nm, melanopsin maximum) most potently suppresses melatonin
- Jet lag: rapid phase shift that SCN takes ~1 day/time zone to correct

## 🔑 REM Sleep Mechanisms

### REM Generation
| Cell type | Location | Role |
|-----------|----------|------|
| **REM-on (cholinergic)** | PPT (pedunculopontine tegmentum) + LDT (laterodorsal tegmentum) | Generate REM; ACh activates thalamus (EEG desynchronization) + promotes EEG activation + drives pontine-geniculo-occipital (PGO) waves (precede eye movements) |
| **REM-off (monoaminergic)** | Locus coeruleus (NE) + dorsal raphe (5-HT) | Suppress REM during wake/NREM; their silencing during REM permits REM expression |
| **Sublaterodorsal nucleus (SLD)** | Dorsolateral pons (REM-on glutamatergic) | Drives atonia pathway; projects to medullary reticular formation → glycinergic/GABAergic interneurons → hyperpolarize ventral horn motor neurons |

### REM Atonia Circuit
- SLD → ventromedial medulla (GABA) + raphe magnus/pallidus → **glycine + GABA onto spinal motor neurons** → hyperpolarization → **muscle atonia**
- Atonia prevents motor behavior during dreams
- **RBD (REM sleep behavior disorder)**: failure of this pathway → motor neurons not inhibited → patients act out dreams (punching, kicking, yelling)

### PGO Waves
- Pontine-geniculo-occipital waves; generated by cholinergic PPT neurons
- Travel: pons → LGN (lateral geniculate nucleus) → occipital cortex
- Precede REM eye movements by ~1 second
- May provide random activation that drives dream content (activation-synthesis hypothesis)

## 🏥 Clinical Significance

| Condition | Pathophysiology | Key Features | Diagnosis / Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)** | Pharyngeal collapse during sleep → repeated airway obstruction → EEG arousals → fragmented sleep; ↓SWS and ↓REM; intermittent hypoxia | Loud snoring; witnessed apneas; excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS); morning headaches; nocturia; obesity (most common risk factor) | Polysomnography (AHI ≥5/h); CPAP first-line; mandibular advancement; surgery for anatomic causes; risks: HTN, CVD, metabolic syndrome, cognitive impairment, stroke |
| **Narcolepsy type 1** | Autoimmune destruction of orexin/hypocretin neurons (lateral hypothalamus); HLA-DQB1*0602 (98% of type 1) | **Tetrad**: EDS + cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion) + hypnagogic/hypnopompic hallucinations + sleep paralysis; REM intrusion into wakefulness | CSF hypocretin-1 <110 pg/mL (diagnostic); MSLT: ≥2 SOREMPs (sleep-onset REM periods), mean sleep latency ≤8 min; treatment: modafinil/armodafinil (EDS), sodium oxybate (cataplexy + EDS), pitolisant (H3 inverse agonist) |
| **Narcolepsy type 2** | Unknown pathophysiology; normal CSF hypocretin | EDS + SOREMPs; no cataplexy; normal CSF orexin | MSLT criteria; no biomarker; treat EDS |
| **Insomnia disorder** | Hyperarousal model: ↑cortical metabolism at night; cognitive/physiological arousal; conditioned arousal | Difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep; daytime impairment; ≥3 nights/week; ≥3 months | **CBT-I** first line (stimulus control, sleep restriction, sleep hygiene, relaxation, cognitive therapy); pharmacotherapy: Z-drugs (zolpidem — GABA-A), doxepin (H1), suvorexant (orexin antagonist), melatonin (sleep onset) |
| **REM sleep behavior disorder (RBD)** | Loss of REM atonia (SLD/atonia circuit failure); **α-synuclein accumulation** in brainstem | Acting out dreams: shouting, punching, kicking during REM; partner injuries; vivid/action-filled dreams | **Polysomnography**: REM without atonia (>50% of REM); **>80% develop synucleinopathy** (Parkinson's, DLB, or MSA) within 10–15 years — prodromal biomarker; clonazepam or melatonin (symptomatic) |
| **Restless legs syndrome (RLS)** | Iron deficiency in basal ganglia (↑arousal from spinal cord dopaminergic circuits); dopaminergic dysfunction | Urge to move legs + uncomfortable sensation, worse at rest and evening, relieved by movement; often with periodic limb movements in sleep (PLMS) | Clinical diagnosis (IRLSSG criteria); check ferritin (treat if <75 µg/L); dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole); α2δ ligands (gabapentin, pregabalin); avoid dopamine augmentation |
| **Fatal familial insomnia (FFI)** | Prion disease; PRNP D178N mutation + Met129 polymorphism; selective degeneration of **thalamic nuclei** (VLPO + mediodorsal + anterior) → loss of sleep generation | Progressive total insomnia → autonomic dysregulation (hyperhidrosis, hyperthermia, tachycardia) → motor dysfunction (ataxia, dysarthria) → stupor → coma → death (months–years) | Autosomal dominant; age of onset 40–60; PSG: ↓total sleep, absent sleep spindles, absent K-complexes; thalamic hypometabolism on FDG-PET; no treatment |
| **Circadian rhythm disorders** | Phase shifts or loss of entrainment | DSPS (delayed sleep phase: teens/young adults); ASPS (advanced: elderly); shift work; jet lag; non-24h sleep-wake (blind individuals) | Light therapy (morning light for DSPS); melatonin; chronotherapy; tasimelteon (non-24h) |
| **Kleine-Levin syndrome** | Unknown (autoimmune?); young males | Recurrent hypersomnia episodes (18–20h/day) + hyperphagia + hypersexuality + cognitive dysfunction lasting days–weeks; normal between episodes | Clinical diagnosis; self-limiting; lithium for prevention |

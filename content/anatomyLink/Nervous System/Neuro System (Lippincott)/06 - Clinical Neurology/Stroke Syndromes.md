# 🧠 Stroke Syndromes

> **Stroke is the sudden onset of focal neurological deficits from a cerebrovascular cause — either ischemic (85%) or hemorrhagic (15%). It is the leading cause of long-term disability and the second leading cause of death worldwide, with 1.9 million neurons lost per minute of untreated ischemic stroke.**

## 🗂️ Key Information

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Ischemic stroke | 85% of all strokes |
| Hemorrhagic stroke | 15% (ICH ~10%, SAH ~5%) |
| Global burden | 2nd leading cause of death worldwide; #1 cause of long-term disability |
| Neuron loss rate | ~1.9 million neurons/minute untreated |
| DWI restriction onset | Within minutes of ischemia (cytotoxic edema) |
| FLAIR positivity | ~6 hours after onset (vasogenic edema) |
| Penumbra window | Variable; up to 24h with perfusion-diffusion mismatch imaging |
| tPA window | Within 4.5 hours of last known well |
| Thrombectomy window | Up to 24 hours (DAWN/DEFUSE-3 criteria) |

## 🔑 Ischemic Stroke — Subtypes (TOAST Classification)

| Subtype | Mechanism | Features |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Large vessel atherothrombotic** | Atherosclerosis at bifurcations (ICA, MCA, basilar); in-situ thrombosis or artery-to-artery embolism | Stepwise progression; TIA precursors; carotid bruit; associated with hypertension, diabetes, smoking |
| **Cardioembolic** | AF (most common), valvular disease (rheumatic MS), LV thrombus (post-MI), PFO, atrial myxoma | Large cortical infarcts; hemorrhagic transformation common (reperfusion into infarcted brain); sudden maximal onset; multiple territory involvement |
| **Small vessel / Lacunar** | Lipohyalinosis or microatheroma of penetrating arteries (lenticulostriate, thalamoperforators, basilar perforators) | Infarcts <15mm; classic syndromes (pure motor, pure sensory, ataxic hemiparesis, dysarthria-clumsy hand); no cortical signs |
| **Cryptogenic** | ~25% of ischemic strokes; no definitive cause found after workup | Often younger patients; consider PFO, occult AF, hypercoagulable state, aortic arch atheroma |
| **Watershed / Hemodynamic** | Hypoperfusion at ACA-MCA or MCA-PCA border zones (internal or cortical) | Internal watershed: arm > leg weakness ("man-in-barrel" syndrome — bilateral proximal arm weakness with spared face and legs); cortical: cortical ribbon infarcts on MRI |

## 🔑 Pathophysiology of Ischemia

| Concept | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Ischemic core** | Zero perfusion; irreversible neuronal death within 4–6 minutes; cytotoxic edema; DWI bright immediately |
| **Ischemic penumbra** | Collateral blood flow maintains viability but not function; electrically silent but structurally intact; **target for reperfusion therapy** |
| **Excitotoxicity** | Ischemia → failure of Na/K-ATPase → membrane depolarization → massive glutamate release → NMDA receptor overactivation → Ca²⁺ influx → mitochondrial dysfunction → caspase activation → apoptosis + necrosis |
| **Cerebral autoregulation** | Normally maintains CBF constant over MAP 60–150 mmHg; impaired in chronic HTN (curve shifted right) and in acute stroke (pressure-passive flow) |
| **Cytotoxic edema** | Intracellular water shift (Na⁺ and water enter cells); begins within minutes; DWI restriction; does not disrupt BBB initially |
| **Vasogenic edema** | BBB breakdown; extracellular water accumulation; FLAIR positive at ~6h; peaks 3–5 days; risk of herniation |
| **Reperfusion injury** | ROS generation, neutrophil infiltration, complement activation upon restoration of blood flow to ischemic tissue |

## 🔑 Cerebral Artery Territory Syndromes

| Artery | Territory | Key Deficits |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| **MCA dominant (superior division)** | Lateral frontal lobe, anterior parietal | Contralateral face + arm > leg weakness and sensory loss; **Broca's aphasia** (non-fluent, effortful speech, intact comprehension, impaired repetition); no visual field defect |
| **MCA dominant (inferior division)** | Posterior temporal, inferior parietal | **Wernicke's aphasia** (fluent, paraphasic, poor comprehension, poor repetition); contralateral **superior quadrantanopia** (Meyer's loop); no hemiparesis |
| **MCA dominant (complete)** | Entire lateral hemisphere | **Global aphasia** (no fluency, no comprehension, no repetition) + contralateral hemiplegia (face = arm = leg) + contralateral homonymous hemianopia; deviation of eyes toward lesion |
| **MCA non-dominant (right)** | Lateral right hemisphere | Contralateral hemiplegia + **hemispatial neglect** (left neglect) + **anosognosia** (denial of deficit) + **constructional apraxia** + dysprosodia |
| **ACA** | Medial frontal and parietal (paracentral lobule) | Contralateral **leg > arm > face** weakness and sensory loss; urinary incontinence; **abulia** (reduced initiative); grasp reflex; alien hand syndrome (medial frontal variant) |
| **PCA** | Occipital lobe, medial temporal, thalamus, midbrain | Contralateral **homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing** (dual blood supply to striate cortex); **Dejerine-Roussy syndrome** (thalamic pain — delayed, burning, allodynic); **memory loss** if hippocampus/fornix involved; ± CN III palsy if midbrain |
| **PICA (Wallenberg / lateral medullary syndrome)** | Lateral medulla (nucleus ambiguus, spinothalamic tract, CN V spinal nucleus, descending sympathetics, inferior cerebellum) | **Ipsilateral**: facial pain/temp loss (CN V nucleus), Horner's (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis — descending sympathetics), limb ataxia (inferior cerebellar peduncle), dysphagia + hoarseness (CN IX/X — nucleus ambiguus); **Contralateral**: body pain/temp loss (lateral spinothalamic); **NO hemiplegia** (corticospinal spared); hiccups; vertigo/nystagmus |
| **AICA (lateral pontine syndrome)** | Lateral pons, anterior-inferior cerebellum; labyrinthine artery branches | **Ipsilateral**: facial weakness (CN VII nucleus), facial sensory loss (CN V), sensorineural hearing loss + vertigo (CN VIII + labyrinthine artery), Horner's; **Contralateral**: body spinothalamic loss; ± abducens (CN VI); clinical clue: sudden unilateral hearing loss + vertigo + facial palsy |
| **SCA (superior cerebellar artery)** | Superior cerebellum, lateral midbrain/pons tegmentum | **Ipsilateral**: limb ataxia, Horner's, CN IV palsy possible; **Contralateral**: face and body pain/temp loss (lateral tegmental spinothalamic + trigeminal); dysarthria |
| **Basilar artery (complete occlusion)** | Bilateral pons, midbrain, cerebellum | **Locked-in syndrome**: quadriplegia + anarthria + preserved consciousness + preserved vertical eye movements and blinking (CN III, IV spared); coma if midbrain reticular formation involved; medical emergency — high mortality without thrombectomy |
| **Basilar tip (top of basilar syndrome)** | Bilateral PCA territory + midbrain + thalami | Bilateral occipital infarction (cortical blindness ± Anton's syndrome — denial of blindness); CN III palsies; **somnolence** (midbrain ARAS); **memory loss** (bilateral thalami/hippocampi); vertical gaze palsy (Parinaud's — rostral midbrain) |
| **Lacunar — internal capsule (posterior limb)** | Corticospinal fibers, posterior limb | **Pure motor hemiparesis**: face = arm = leg equally; NO cortical signs (no aphasia, no neglect, no hemianopia) |
| **Lacunar — thalamus (VPL)** | VPL nucleus | **Pure sensory stroke**: contralateral hemisensory loss (all modalities or dissociated); may evolve to thalamic pain (Dejerine-Roussy) |
| **Lacunar — pons (basis pontis)** | Pontine base | **Dysarthria-clumsy hand syndrome**: slurred speech + ipsilateral hand clumsiness/weakness without ataxia |
| **Lacunar — internal capsule/pons** | Corticospinal + cerebellar | **Ataxic hemiparesis**: contralateral leg weakness + ipsilateral limb ataxia disproportionate to weakness |

## 🔑 Hemorrhagic Stroke

### Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)

| Location | % of ICH | Cause | Key Features |
|----------|---------|-------|--------------|
| **Putamen** | 35% (most common) | HTN → lenticulostriate rupture | Contralateral hemiplegia, hemisensory loss, eye deviation toward lesion |
| **Thalamus** | 20% | HTN → thalamoperforators | Contralateral hemisensory loss, eye deviation downward-inward ("thalamic gaze"), small reactive pupils |
| **Pons** | 10% | HTN → basilar perforators | Pinpoint pupils, loss of horizontal gaze, quadriplegia, coma; very high mortality |
| **Cerebellum** | 10% | HTN | Sudden occipital headache, ataxia, vomiting, gaze palsy; **neurosurgical emergency** if >3cm (obstructive hydrocephalus, brainstem compression) |
| **Lobar** | 20% | Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) — elderly; AVM, anticoagulation, cocaine | Recurrent lobar bleeds; GRE/SWI shows cortical microbleeds; Boston criteria for CAA |

- **Cushing's response** (Cushing's reflex): severe ICH → raised ICP → hypertension + bradycardia + irregular respirations (Cushing's triad) — brainstem compression signal
- **Hematoma expansion**: occurs in 30–40% within first 24h; CT spot sign (contrast extravasation) predicts expansion

### Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Classic presentation | Sudden **"thunderclap headache"** — worst headache of life; onset at exertion or Valsalva; meningismus, photophobia, nausea/vomiting |
| Most common cause | Ruptured **saccular (Berry) aneurysm** (85%) at arterial bifurcations |
| CT sensitivity | 98% within first 6h; falls to ~85–90% at 24h |
| LP finding | **Xanthochromia** (yellow CSF from RBC lysis); begins 2–4h after SAH, persists up to 2 weeks; differentiates SAH from traumatic tap |

**Aneurysm Locations and Neurological Clues:**

| Aneurysm Site | % of SAH | Key Clinical Sign |
|--------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Anterior communicating artery (AComm)** | 30–35% (most common) | Abulia, memory loss (A1/Heubner territory); may be asymptomatic pre-rupture |
| **MCA bifurcation** | 20% | Contralateral hemiplegia + aphasia (dominant) or neglect (non-dominant) |
| **Posterior communicating artery (PComm)** | 15–20% | **CN III palsy**: complete ptosis + mydriasis (pupil-involving) + "down and out" eye — surgical emergency (compression = posterior aneurysm, not DM) |
| **Basilar tip** | 5–10% | Top of basilar syndrome; CN III palsy |
| **PICA / vertebral** | 5% | Lateral medullary or cerebellar signs |

**SAH Complications:**

| Complication | Timing | Prevention/Treatment |
|-------------|--------|---------------------|
| **Rebleeding** | First 24–72h (peak day 1); 20–30% if untreated | Early aneurysm securing (clipping or coiling) |
| **Vasospasm** | Days 4–14 (peaks day 7–10) | **Nimodipine** (oral L-type CCB — class I evidence); induced hypertension, hypervolemia; TCD monitoring |
| **Hydrocephalus (acute)** | First hours to days; obstructive (blood in ventricles) | EVD (external ventricular drain) |
| **Hydrocephalus (late)** | Weeks; communicating (impaired CSF reabsorption at arachnoid granulations) | VP shunt |
| **Hyponatremia** | Days 3–10; cerebral salt wasting (more common) vs SIADH | CSWS: replace Na⁺ + volume; SIADH: fluid restrict |

### Other Hemorrhage Types

| Type | Cause | CT Appearance | Key Features |
|------|-------|---------------|--------------|
| **Epidural hematoma** | Middle meningeal artery rupture (temporal bone fracture — pterion is thinnest) | **Biconvex/lenticular**; does not cross suture lines | Classic **lucid interval** then rapid herniation; neurosurgical emergency; most commonly temporal |
| **Acute subdural hematoma** | Bridging vein rupture; high-velocity trauma | **Crescent-shaped**; crosses suture lines; **hyperdense** | Rapid neurological decline; elderly and anticoagulated patients at risk even with minor trauma |
| **Chronic subdural hematoma** | Bridging vein rupture; minor or absent trauma; elderly, anticoagulated | Crescent-shaped; **hypodense or isodense** (3–6 weeks after) | Headache, confusion, hemiparesis; may mimic dementia |

## 🔑 Herniation Syndromes

| Type | Cause | Direction of Displacement | Key Signs |
|------|-------|--------------------------|-----------|
| **Transtentorial (uncal)** | Supratentorial mass pushes uncus of temporal lobe through tentorial notch | Medial temporal → compresses CN III + midbrain | Ipsilateral mydriasis + ptosis (CN III compressed) → contralateral hemiplegia (cerebral peduncle) → bilateral decorticate/decerebrate posturing; Kernohan's notch (contralateral peduncle compression → false localizing ipsilateral weakness) |
| **Central (transtentorial)** | Bilateral supratentorial masses or diffuse cerebral edema | Downward through tentorium | Sequential: diencephalon (small pupils, Cheyne-Stokes) → midbrain (midrange fixed pupils, hyperventilation) → pons (pinpoint pupils, apneustic) → medulla (ataxic breathing, death) |
| **Tonsillar** | Posterior fossa mass or diffuse edema pushes cerebellar tonsils through foramen magnum | Downward | Sudden respiratory arrest, bradycardia (compression of medullary respiratory/cardiovascular centers); neck stiffness |
| **Subfalcine (cingulate)** | Unilateral supratentorial mass pushes cingulate gyrus under falx cerebri | Lateral, under falx | ACA compression → contralateral leg weakness; may be asymptomatic or subtle |
| **Upward (transtentorial)** | Posterior fossa mass pushes cerebellum upward through tentorium | Upward through tentorial notch | Compression of midbrain from below; obstructive hydrocephalus; loss of upward gaze |

## 🔑 Treatment

### Acute Ischemic Stroke

| Treatment | Details | Key Criteria / Contraindications |
|-----------|---------|----------------------------------|
| **IV alteplase (tPA)** | 0.9 mg/kg IV (max 90mg); 10% bolus, remainder over 60 min | Window: **≤4.5h** from last known well; contraindications: intracranial hemorrhage, prior stroke <3 months, BP >185/110 (must treat first), INR >1.7, plt <100k, major surgery <14d, serum glucose <50 or >400 |
| **Mechanical thrombectomy** | Stent retriever or aspiration via femoral/radial access | Window: up to **24h** with DWI-PWI mismatch (DAWN trial: 6–24h; DEFUSE-3: 6–16h); requires large vessel occlusion (ICA, M1, M2, basilar); no hemorrhage |
| **Aspirin** | 325mg within 24–48h (not with tPA for 24h) | Secondary prevention starts immediately after tPA window closes |
| **Anticoagulation (AF)** | Start after 2–14 days depending on infarct size and hemorrhagic transformation risk | Not started immediately in acute phase; NOAC preferred (apixaban, rivaroxaban) |
| **Blood pressure** | Permissive HTN (allow up to 220/120) unless tPA given (target <185/110 before, <180/105 after) | Aggressive lowering harmful in acute ischemic stroke |

### Acute Hemorrhagic Stroke

| Treatment | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Blood pressure control (ICH)** | Target SBP <140 mmHg (ATACH-2, INTERACT-2 trials); intensive lowering to <140 reduces hematoma expansion |
| **Anticoagulation reversal** | Warfarin: 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) + vitamin K; Dabigatran: **idarucizumab** (Praxbind); Factor Xa inhibitors: andexanet alfa |
| **Surgical evacuation (ICH)** | Cerebellar ICH >3cm or with hydrocephalus/brainstem compression; some superficial lobar ICH; not routinely for basal ganglia/thalamic ICH (STICH trials) |
| **ICP management** | HOB 30°, osmotherapy (mannitol or hypertonic saline), sedation, EVD, decompressive craniectomy for malignant MCA infarction (hemicraniectomy — DESTINY, HAMLET trials; age <60) |
| **SAH-specific** | Early aneurysm securing (microsurgical clipping vs. endovascular coiling — ISAT trial: coiling superior for appropriate anatomy); nimodipine 60mg q4h for 21 days |

### Secondary Prevention

| Strategy | Indication |
|----------|-----------|
| Antiplatelet (aspirin ± clopidogrel) | Non-cardioembolic ischemic stroke; dual antiplatelet short-term (21 days) for minor stroke/TIA (POINT/CHANCE trials) |
| High-intensity statin | All ischemic stroke/TIA regardless of LDL; atorvastatin 80mg (SPARCL trial) |
| Antihypertensives | Target BP <130/80; ACEi + thiazide first-line post-stroke |
| Anticoagulation | Cardioembolic stroke (AF, valvular); NOAC preferred over warfarin |
| Carotid endarterectomy (CEA) | Symptomatic carotid stenosis **>70%** (NASCET criteria); best within 2 weeks of event; CAS alternative for high surgical risk |

## 🔑 Stroke Mimics

| Mimic | Key Differentiator |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Seizure (Todd's paralysis)** | Postictal focal weakness; history of seizure or witnessed event; clears within hours; normal MRI acutely |
| **Hypoglycemia** | Always check glucose first; focal deficits at glucose <50; rapid resolution with glucose administration |
| **Hemiplegic migraine** | Young patient; prior similar events; headache accompanies motor/sensory aura; FHM gene mutations (CACNA1A, ATP1A2) |
| **Hypertensive encephalopathy / PRES** | Severe HTN + altered consciousness + seizures + cortical visual loss; MRI: posterior white matter FLAIR hyperintensity (vasogenic edema); reversible |
| **MS relapse** | Young patient; prior episodes; MRI: white matter lesions typical for MS (periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord); CSF oligoclonal bands |
| **Brain tumor** | Gradual onset (usually); seizure at onset common; MRI: mass effect, ring enhancement (GBM/metastasis); surrounding vasogenic edema |
| **Conversion disorder** | Inconsistent exam (Hoover's sign, collapsing weakness); normal imaging; psychiatric history; not a diagnosis of exclusion — use positive features |

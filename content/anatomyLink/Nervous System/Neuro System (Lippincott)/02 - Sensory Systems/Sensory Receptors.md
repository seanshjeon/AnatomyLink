# 🖐️ Sensory Receptors

> Sensory receptors are **specialized structures that transduce specific forms of energy (mechanical, thermal, chemical, electromagnetic) into graded electrical potentials**, which, when sufficient, trigger action potentials in afferent neurons and transmit sensory information to the CNS via the **labeled line principle**.

## 🗂️ Key Information
| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Definition | Specialized endings or cells that detect specific stimuli |
| Transduction | Conversion of stimulus energy → receptor (generator) potential |
| Receptor potential | Graded, local, NOT all-or-nothing; amplitude proportional to stimulus intensity |
| Location | Skin, muscle, tendons, joints, viscera, special sense organs |
| Fiber types | Aα (Ia/Ib), Aβ (II), Aδ (III), C (IV) |
| Primary afferent soma | Dorsal root ganglion (spinal) or trigeminal ganglion (face) |

## 🔑 Classification by Stimulus Type

| Receptor Class | Stimulus | Fiber Type | Location/Example |
|----------------|----------|------------|------------------|
| **Mechanoreceptors** | Mechanical deformation (touch, pressure, vibration, stretch) | Aα, Aβ | Skin, muscle spindles, GTOs, joint capsules |
| **Thermoreceptors** | Temperature change | Aδ (cold: TRPM8), C (warm: TRPV1-3) | Free nerve endings in skin |
| **Nociceptors** | Noxious mechanical, thermal, chemical stimuli | Aδ (fast pain), C (slow pain) | Free nerve endings, widespread |
| **Proprioceptors** | Body position, muscle length, tension | Aα (Ia, Ib), Aβ (II) | Muscle spindles, GTOs, joint receptors |
| **Chemoreceptors** | Chemical ligands (O₂, CO₂, pH, odorants, taste) | Varies | Carotid body, olfactory epithelium, taste buds |
| **Photoreceptors** | Electromagnetic radiation (visible light) | N/A (specialized cells) | Rods and cones, retina |
| **Osmoreceptors** | Osmolarity changes | Hypothalamic neurons | Hypothalamus (supraoptic nucleus) |

## 🔑 Classification by Adaptation Rate

Adaptation is the decline in receptor firing despite maintained stimulus. Rate of adaptation distinguishes four major cutaneous mechanoreceptors:

### Rapidly Adapting (RA) Receptors
Fire at stimulus onset and offset only; detect change, movement, and transient stimuli.

| Receptor | Adaptation | Stimulus | Receptive Field | Fiber | Depth |
|----------|------------|----------|-----------------|-------|-------|
| **Meissner's corpuscles** (RA1) | Rapidly adapting | Light touch, slip detection, texture, low-frequency vibration (5–40 Hz) | Small, sharp borders | Aβ | Superficial dermis (dermal papillae) |
| **Pacinian corpuscles** (RA2) | Rapidly adapting | High-frequency vibration (250–300 Hz), deep pressure, transmitted vibration | Large, diffuse | Aβ | Deep dermis, periosteum, mesentery |

> **Meissner's corpuscles** are concentrated in fingertips, lips, and palms — critical for fine texture discrimination and object manipulation. Density decreases markedly with age.

> **Pacinian corpuscles** respond best to vibration at ~250 Hz — the frequency most efficiently transmitted through hard surfaces (e.g., tool use). Their multilayered lamellar structure acts as a mechanical filter, attenuating static forces.

### Slowly Adapting (SA) Receptors
Maintain firing throughout sustained stimulus; detect steady pressure and static position.

| Receptor | Adaptation | Stimulus | Receptive Field | Fiber | Depth |
|----------|------------|----------|-----------------|-------|-------|
| **Merkel's discs** (SA1) | Slowly adapting | Sustained pressure, fine spatial detail, 2-point discrimination, edges | Small, sharp borders | Aβ | Epidermis (stratum basale) |
| **Ruffini endings** (SA2) | Slowly adapting | Skin stretch, lateral forces, joint position, finger position | Large, diffuse | Aβ | Deep dermis, joint capsules |

> **Merkel's discs** are the primary receptor for fine spatial acuity and 2-point discrimination. High density in fingertips (~100/cm²). Critical for Braille reading.

> **Ruffini endings** signal skin stretch direction and contribute to joint position sense alongside proprioceptors. Relatively insensitive to direct indentation.

## 🔑 Free Nerve Endings

- Most numerous sensory ending type in the body
- Unmyelinated (C fibers) or thinly myelinated (Aδ fibers)
- Found in: skin (epidermis and dermis), cornea, dental pulp, viscera, meninges, periosteum

| Subtype | Fiber | Stimulus | Sensation |
|---------|-------|----------|-----------|
| Mechanical nociceptors | Aδ | High-threshold mechanical | Sharp, fast pain |
| Thermal nociceptors (heat) | Aδ, C | >45°C (TRPV1), <15°C (TRPM8) | Burning, cold pain |
| Polymodal nociceptors | C | Mechanical + thermal + chemical | Slow, aching, burning pain |
| Warm receptors | C | 30–46°C | Warmth (not painful) |
| Cold receptors | Aδ | 10–35°C (TRPM8) | Coolness |

## 🔑 Receptor (Generator) Potential

The receptor potential is the fundamental electrical event of sensory transduction:

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Nature | Graded depolarization (or hyperpolarization in photoreceptors) |
| All-or-nothing? | No — amplitude scales with stimulus intensity |
| Location | At receptor ending (specialized membrane) |
| Mechanism | Stimulus opens/closes ion channels → current flow → local depolarization |
| Threshold | If receptor potential reaches threshold at spike-initiation zone → AP generated |
| Frequency coding | Larger stimulus → larger receptor potential → higher AP frequency (rate coding) |
| Generator potential | Synonym; emphasizes its role in generating APs in the afferent axon |

> Photoreceptors are exceptional: they **hyperpolarize** in response to light (dark current model — cGMP-gated cation channels close in response to light-activated phosphodiesterase cascade).

## 🔑 Sensory Transduction

Transduction converts stimulus energy to electrical signal via ion channel opening:

| Modality | Transduction Mechanism | Key Channels/Molecules |
|----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Mechanical touch/pressure | Deformation → mechanosensitive channels open | Piezo1, Piezo2 (most cutaneous mechanoreceptors) |
| Pain/heat (>45°C) | Capsaicin, heat, protons → channel opening | TRPV1 (transient receptor potential vanilloid 1) |
| Cold (<15°C) | Cool temperatures, menthol → channel opening | TRPM8 |
| Moderate cold (15–25°C) | Channel opening | TRPA1 |
| Proprioception (muscle stretch) | Stretch → mechanosensitive channels in Ia endings | Piezo2, KCNQ4 |
| Vision | Photon → rhodopsin → cGMP hydrolysis → channel closure | cGMP-gated channels, opsin |
| Olfaction | Odorant → GPCR → cAMP → channel opening | CNG channels |

## 🔑 Coding Principles

### Labeled Line Principle
- Each sensory modality is carried by a dedicated ("labeled") neural pathway
- The quality of sensation is determined by which pathway is activated, NOT the nature of the stimulus itself
- Example: electrical stimulation of the optic nerve → perceived as light (photism), regardless of stimulus type
- Supports discrete receptor-specific pathways for touch, pain, temperature, etc.

### Population Coding
- Sensation arises from the pattern of activity across a **population** of neurons with overlapping receptive fields
- No single neuron uniquely encodes a stimulus — the ensemble pattern conveys identity
- Important for fine discrimination of direction, texture, and complex shapes
- Underlies across-fiber pattern theory for taste and olfaction

## 🔑 Receptive Fields and Spatial Acuity

A **receptive field** is the area of skin (or sensory surface) where stimulation activates a given afferent neuron.

| Body Region | Receptive Field Size | Two-Point Discrimination Threshold | Dominant Receptor |
|-------------|---------------------|------------------------------------|-------------------|
| Fingertip | Very small (~2–4 mm) | 2–3 mm | Merkel's discs, Meissner's corpuscles |
| Palm | Small–medium | ~10 mm | Merkel's discs |
| Lips | Small | ~5 mm | Meissner's corpuscles |
| Forearm | Medium | ~20–30 mm | Merkel's discs |
| Back | Large | ~40–70 mm | Merkel's discs (sparse) |
| Thigh | Large | ~40 mm | Sparse distribution |

> **Inverse relationship**: Larger receptive fields → lower spatial acuity (worse 2-point discrimination). Fingertips have the smallest fields and highest cortical representation (homunculus).

**Two-point discrimination** is a clinical measure of innervation density and receptive field size. It tests the minimum distance at which two simultaneous skin contacts can be perceived as separate points.

## 🔑 Sensory Fiber Classification

| Fiber Class | Diameter | Conduction Velocity | Myelination | Modality |
|-------------|----------|---------------------|-------------|----------|
| Aα (Ia/Ib) | 13–20 µm | 70–120 m/s | Heavily myelinated | Muscle spindle afferents (Ia), GTOs (Ib) |
| Aβ (II) | 6–12 µm | 33–75 m/s | Myelinated | Cutaneous mechanoreceptors (touch, pressure, vibration) |
| Aδ (III) | 1–5 µm | 5–30 m/s | Thinly myelinated | Fast pain, cold, crude touch |
| C (IV) | 0.2–1.5 µm | 0.5–2 m/s | Unmyelinated | Slow pain, warmth, itch, postganglionic sympathetic |

## 🏥 Clinical Significance

| Condition | Receptor/Fiber Affected | Clinical Features |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Diabetic peripheral neuropathy** (large fiber loss) | Aβ fibers (mechanoreceptors, proprioceptors) | Loss of vibration sense, proprioception; positive Romberg; painless foot ulcers |
| **Diabetic peripheral neuropathy** (small fiber loss) | Aδ, C fibers (nociceptors, thermoreceptors) | Loss of pain and temperature; burning dysesthesias; autonomic dysfunction |
| **Leprosy** (*Mycobacterium leprae*) | Preferentially damages cooler peripheral nerves (Aδ, C) | Selective loss of pain and temperature; preserved proprioception and vibration; "glove-and-stocking" anesthesia; trophic ulcers |
| **Carpal tunnel syndrome** | Median nerve compression (Aβ fibers primarily) | Numbness and tingling in lateral 3½ digits; loss of 2-point discrimination; thenar wasting if severe; Tinel's and Phalen's signs |
| **Friedreich's ataxia** | Dorsal root ganglion (large myelinated fibers) | Loss of proprioception and vibration; sensory ataxia; absent deep tendon reflexes |
| **Aging** | Meissner's corpuscles (density ↓ ~50% by age 70) | Reduced light-touch sensitivity; impaired texture discrimination; increased fall risk |
| **Syringomyelia** | Central cord → interrupts decussating Aδ/C fibers | Cape distribution loss of pain and temperature; preserved large-fiber modalities |

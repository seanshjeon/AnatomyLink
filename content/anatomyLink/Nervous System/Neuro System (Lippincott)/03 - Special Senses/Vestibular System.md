# 🔄 Vestibular System

> The vestibular system is the **primary sensory system for detecting head motion and orientation in space**, using mechanosensory hair cells in the semicircular canals (angular acceleration) and otolith organs (linear acceleration and gravity). It drives compensatory reflexes for gaze stabilization (VOR), postural control (VST), and contributes to the perception of spatial orientation and self-motion.

## 🔑 Vestibular Apparatus: Structure and Function

### Bony and Membranous Labyrinth

- The inner ear is housed in the **petrous temporal bone**
- **Bony labyrinth** contains perilymph (high Na⁺, low K⁺)
- **Membranous labyrinth** is suspended within bony labyrinth and contains **endolymph** (high K⁺, low Na⁺); same fluid as cochlear scala media

### Semicircular Canals (SCCs)

| Canal | Plane of Detection | Orientation | Paired Canal (Push-Pull) |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Horizontal (lateral)** | Yaw (head turns left/right) | ~30° tilted up from horizontal when head upright | Contralateral horizontal canal |
| **Anterior (superior)** | Pitch (nodding) and roll | Vertical; angled ~45° toward nose | Contralateral posterior canal |
| **Posterior** | Pitch and roll | Vertical; angled ~45° away from nose | Contralateral anterior canal |

**Ampulla and Crista Ampullaris**:
- Each canal has an **ampulla** at one end containing the **crista ampullaris** (sensory organ)
- Crista = ridge of hair cells embedded in a **cupula** (gelatinous dome) that spans the full width of the ampulla
- **Cupula is neutrally buoyant** (same density as endolymph) — does not respond to static head position, only to angular acceleration
- Angular acceleration → endolymph lags behind canal walls (inertia) → **relative endolymph flow** → cupula deflected → hair cells deflected

### Otolith Organs

| Organ | Orientation | Function |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Utricle** | Macula horizontal (when head upright) | Detects **horizontal linear acceleration** and **static head tilt from vertical** (gravity component) |
| **Saccule** | Macula vertical (when head upright) | Detects **vertical linear acceleration** (e.g., elevator) and **static tilt** |

**Macula Structure**:
- Hair cells embedded in gelatinous **otolithic membrane** topped by **otoconia** (calcium carbonate crystals, also called otoliths)
- Otoconia are denser than surrounding fluid → when head tilts, gravity displaces otolithic membrane → shear force on hair cells → deflection
- **Striola**: curved line dividing macula into two regions with opposite hair cell polarities (enables bidirectional sensitivity)


## 🔑 Hair Cell Mechanotransduction

### Type I vs. Type II Hair Cells

| Property | Type I | Type II |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Shape | Flask-shaped | Cylindrical |
| Afferent synapse | Large calyx (cup-shaped) terminal | Bouton synapses |
| Location | Central crista and macula | Peripheral crista and macula |
| Response | Irregular discharge; fast adaptation | Regular discharge; slower adaptation |

### Transduction Mechanism

1. **Kinocilium** = single true cilium at tall edge of stereocilia bundle; establishes **morphological polarization** (direction of excitation)
2. **Stereocilia** deflect **toward kinocilium** → **tip links** (fine filaments connecting stereocilia tips) are stretched → pull open **MET channels** → **K⁺ and Ca²⁺ influx** → **depolarization** → ↑glutamate release → ↑afferent firing rate
3. Deflection **away from kinocilium** → tip links compressed → MET channels close → **hyperpolarization** → ↓afferent firing rate
4. Resting (spontaneous) firing rate ~80–100 spikes/sec — allows bidirectional modulation

### Push-Pull Organization

Paired semicircular canals work in opposition to maximize sensitivity and cancel common-mode noise:

**Example: head turns LEFT**
| Canal | Endolymph Flow Relative to Canal | Cupula Direction | Firing Rate |
|-------|----------------------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Left horizontal | Ampullopetal (toward ampulla) | Toward kinocilium | **Increases** |
| Right horizontal | Ampullofugal (away from ampulla) | Away from kinocilium | **Decreases** |

> The brain compares signals from paired canals — the difference signal is more robust than either alone. This also allows the CNS to detect which canal is lesioned: loss of one side causes asymmetry, perceived as rotation.


## 🔑 Vestibular Pathways

### Four Vestibular Nuclei (Medulla-Pons Junction)

| Nucleus | Location | Major Projections | Key Function |
|---------|----------|------------------|-------------|
| **Superior (Bechterew's)** | Pons | MLF → CN III/IV; cerebellar flocculus | Angular VOR (head rotation) |
| **Medial (Schwalbe's)** | Medulla-pons | Bilateral MLF → CN III/IV/VI; ipsilateral descending MLF → cervical cord | Angular VOR; cervicocollic reflex (VSR); commissural inhibition |
| **Lateral (Deiters')** | Medulla | Lateral vestibulospinal tract (LVST) → ipsilateral spinal cord | **Postural reflexes** (antigravity muscles) |
| **Inferior (Roller's/descending)** | Medulla | Spinal cord; cerebellum; brainstem reticular formation | Linear VOR; cerebellar integration |

### Major Output Pathways from Vestibular Nuclei

| Pathway | Route | Function |
|---------|-------|---------|
| **VOR pathway** | Medial vestibular nucleus → **MLF (medial longitudinal fasciculus)** → contralateral CN VI nucleus (abducens) → abducens motor neurons + interneurons → contralateral CN III (via MLF) → medial rectus | Compensatory eye movements opposite to head movement; gaze stabilization |
| **Lateral vestibulospinal tract (LVST)** | Lateral (Deiters') nucleus → descends uncrossed in anterior cord → **ipsilateral antigravity muscles** (extensors in limbs, axial muscles) | Postural control; righting reflexes; fall prevention |
| **Medial vestibulospinal tract (MVST)** | Medial nucleus → descends bilaterally in MLF → cervical and upper thoracic cord → neck/upper extremity muscles | Head stabilization (cervicocollic reflex) |
| **Cerebellar projections** | All nuclei → flocculonodular lobe (nodulus + flocculus); also to uvula and fastigial nucleus | VOR adaptation, gain calibration, postural learning |
| **Thalamocortical projections** | Via posterolateral thalamus (VP, posterior group) → PIVC (posterior insula), area 3a, temporoparietal junction | Conscious perception of motion, verticality, spatial orientation |


## 🔑 Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex (VOR)

### Three-Neuron Arc

1. **Primary afferent** (CN VIII) → vestibular nucleus
2. **Second-order interneuron** (e.g., medial vestibular nucleus) → crosses in pons → contralateral abducens (CN VI) nucleus
3. **Motor neuron** (CN VI → lateral rectus; CN VI interneuron → CN III → medial rectus)

> For leftward head rotation: left CN VIII fires ↑ → right CN VI fires → right lateral rectus + left medial rectus contract → eyes move right (compensating for leftward head movement). Simultaneously, right CN VIII fires ↓ via inhibitory commissural connections.

### VOR Parameters

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Gain** | Eye velocity / head velocity; normal = 1.0 (eyes move exactly opposite to head) |
| **Latency** | ~10 ms (one of the fastest reflexes in the body; smooth pursuit has ~80 ms latency) |
| **Frequency range** | Effective for high-frequency head movements (0.5–5 Hz and beyond); smooth pursuit handles low frequency |
| Gain < 1.0 | Peripheral vestibular lesion or cerebellar lesion; **Head Impulse Test positive** (corrective saccade needed after brief head thrust) |

### Clinical Tests of VOR

| Test | Method | Normal | Abnormal |
|------|--------|--------|---------|
| **Head Impulse Test (HIT / Head Thrust Test)** | Brief, high-velocity, small-amplitude head thrust; observe eyes | No corrective saccade (gaze stays on target) | Corrective saccade seen = ipsilateral peripheral vestibular loss |
| **Doll's Eye (oculocephalic) reflex** | Passive head rotation while patient fixes on examiner; used in coma | Eyes deviate opposite to head rotation (VOR intact) | Eyes move with head = absent VOR = severe brainstem lesion at pontine level |
| **Caloric testing** | Cool (30°C) or warm (44°C) water/air in ear canal | COWS: **Cold Opposite, Warm Same** — nystagmus fast phase direction | Absent response = ipsilateral peripheral or central loss |
| **Rotary chair testing** | Sinusoidal rotation; measures VOR gain/phase | Symmetric responses | Gain asymmetry; abnormal phase |


## 🔑 Nystagmus

- **Definition**: involuntary rhythmic eye movement with slow drift + fast corrective phase (saccade)
- **Convention**: named by direction of **fast phase**
- **Slow phase** = VOR-driven (pathological drift); fast phase = brainstem-generated corrective saccade

| Type | Fast Phase | Example Cause |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| Peripheral (canal) nystagmus | Beats **away from lesion** (toward intact side) | Vestibular neuritis (fast phase to intact side), post-caloric |
| Direction-changing (gaze-evoked) | Changes with gaze direction | Central (cerebellar or brainstem), drug effect |
| Upbeat | Upward fast phase | Central (pontomedullary or periaqueductal gray) |
| Downbeat | Downward fast phase | Craniocervical junction lesion (Chiari malformation), cerebellar |
| Torsional | Rotating fast phase | BPPV (posterior canal variant), central |
| Optokinetic | Follows moving striped pattern | Tests parieto-occipital pursuit system |


## 🏥 Clinical Significance

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Features | Diagnosis/Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)** | Free-floating otoconia from utricle enter posterior SCC (most common) → simulate angular acceleration | Brief (10–60 sec) intense rotatory vertigo triggered by head position changes (lying down, rolling over, looking up); **Dix-Hallpike maneuver** positive (geotropic torsional nystagmus with latency + fatiguability) | **Epley maneuver** (canalith repositioning) — highly effective; Semont maneuver; horizontal canal variant: supine roll test |
| **Vestibular neuritis** | Unilateral destruction of vestibular nerve (viral — HSV-1 reactivation proposed) or end-organ; cochlear branch spared | Sudden onset prolonged vertigo (days), no hearing loss, no tinnitus; horizontal-torsional nystagmus fast phase **away from lesion**; HIT positive ipsilateral; VOR gain reduced | Supportive (vestibulosuppressants short-term); corticosteroids (controversial); **vestibular rehabilitation** (key for compensation) |
| **Labyrinthitis** | Inner ear infection (bacterial or viral) affecting both cochlear and vestibular end organs | Vertigo + hearing loss + tinnitus; can follow otitis media or viral URI | Treat underlying infection; supportive; permanent SNHL possible |
| **Menière's disease** | Endolymphatic hydrops (distension of endolymph-filled spaces; exact mechanism unclear — possible impaired endolymph resorption) | Episodic: rotatory vertigo (20 min–4 hr) + fluctuating low-frequency SNHL + roaring tinnitus + aural fullness; eventually permanent SNHL | Low-sodium diet; diuretics; intratympanic steroids or gentamicin; endolymphatic sac decompression |
| **Acoustic neuroma (Vestibular schwannoma)** | Benign CN VIII Schwann cell tumor, primarily superior vestibular nerve | Gradual unilateral SNHL + tinnitus; vertigo often mild (gradual compensation); CPA syndrome; bilateral = NF2 (merlin/schwannomin, chr 22q12) | MRI with gadolinium (gold standard); surgery vs. radiosurgery vs. observation |
| **Central vertigo** | Brainstem (medullary/pontine) or cerebellar lesion affecting vestibular nuclei or pathways | Vertical or direction-changing nystagmus; no tinnitus/hearing loss typically; other brainstem signs (diplopia, dysphagia, ataxia); HINTS exam: HIT negative + INO/skew + direction-changing nystagmus = central | MRI brain; treat underlying cause (stroke, MS, tumor) |
| **Inferior cerebellar infarct (PICA territory — Wallenberg syndrome)** | Lateral medullary infarct; may involve vestibular nuclei | Vertigo + ipsilateral Horner's + ipsilateral facial numbness + contralateral limb hemianesthesia + dysphagia/dysphonia + ipsilateral ataxia | MRI DWI; antiplatelet/anticoagulation |

### HINTS Exam (differentiating peripheral from central in acute vestibular syndrome)

| Test | Peripheral Finding | Central Finding (red flag) |
|------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **H**ead Impulse Test | **Positive** (corrective saccade) | **Negative** (normal VOR despite vertigo = central) |
| **IN**direction of nystagmus | **Unidirectional**, horizontal-torsional | **Direction-changing** with gaze |
| **T**est of Skew | **Absent** vertical skew deviation | **Present** vertical skew deviation (brainstem) |

> A normal HIT in a vertiginous patient is a RED FLAG for central pathology (stroke).

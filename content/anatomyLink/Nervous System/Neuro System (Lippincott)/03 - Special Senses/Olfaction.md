# 👃 Olfaction

> The olfactory system is the **phylogenetically oldest sensory system**, unique among all sensory modalities in that its primary afferents project **directly to cortex without a thalamic relay**. Olfactory sensory neurons (OSNs) are remarkable as the **only CNS neurons that are directly exposed to the environment and that are regularly replaced** throughout adult life from basal stem cells, making them a model for adult neurogenesis.

## 🔗 Key Connections
- [[Gustation]] — combined olfactory + taste signals create flavor perception
- [[Limbic System]] — direct olfactory projections to amygdala and hippocampus
- [[Hippocampus]] — olfactory memory consolidation; Lewy body deposition in neurodegeneration
- [[Amygdala]] — emotional response to odors; fear conditioning
- [[Cranial Nerves]] — CN I (olfactory nerve, technically a CNS tract)
- [[Skull]] — cribriform plate of ethmoid bone (site of CN I transit)

## 🗂️ Key Information
| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Stimulus | Volatile chemical odorants (must dissolve in olfactory mucus) |
| Receptor neurons | Olfactory sensory neurons (OSNs) — bipolar, unmyelinated, ~10 million in humans |
| Cranial nerve | CN I (olfactory nerve) — composed of ~20 fascicles of unmyelinated OSN axons |
| Unique feature 1 | Only sensory system with **no thalamic relay** to primary cortex |
| Unique feature 2 | OSNs are the **only neurons in the CNS that regenerate** (basal stem cell replacement, ~30–60 day cycle) |
| Unique feature 3 | OSNs are **directly exposed to environment** (mucosa) |
| Number of odorant receptors | ~400 functional genes in humans (~1,000 in rodents); largest GPCR gene family |
| Primary cortex | Piriform cortex (paleocortex — 3-layered archicortex) |

## 🔑 Olfactory Epithelium

### Location and Structure

- Located in the **superior nasal septum and superior nasal conchae** — olfactory cleft (~2 cm² in humans; only ~3–5% of total nasal mucosa)
- **Pseudostratified columnar epithelium** containing three main cell types:

| Cell Type | Characteristics | Function |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| **Olfactory sensory neurons (OSNs)** | Bipolar neurons; 6–8 µm soma; single non-motile modified ciliated dendrite extends to surface; unmyelinated axons bundle to form CN I fascicles | Primary olfactory transducers; express single odorant receptor (OR) type; replaced every 30–60 days from basal cells |
| **Sustentacular (supporting) cells** | Tall columnar cells with microvilli; extend full height of epithelium | Physical support; K⁺/Cl⁻ buffering of extracellular space; phagocytosis of debris; detoxification (CYP450 enzymes) |
| **Basal stem cells** (horizontal + globose) | Progenitor cells at epithelial base | Regenerate OSNs and sustentacular cells throughout life; globose basal cells are direct neuronal progenitors |
| **Bowman's glands** | Serous gland cells in lamina propria | Produce olfactory mucus containing **odorant-binding proteins (OBPs)** that concentrate odorants at OR surface; IgA secretion |

### Olfactory Mucus

- Depth ~20–50 µm; odorants must dissolve here before reaching ORs
- Contains: odorant-binding proteins, mucins, IgA, lysozyme, lactoferrin
- Odorant-binding proteins (OBPs): lipocalin family; may concentrate hydrophobic odorants, help odorant reach receptor, and assist in clearance

## 🔑 Olfactory Transduction

### Odorant Receptor (OR) Specificity

- Each OSN expresses **exactly ONE functional OR gene** (from one allele of one gene) — the "one receptor — one neuron" rule (Axel & Buck, Nobel 2004)
- ~400 functional OR genes in humans; ~600 pseudogenes (vs. ~1,000 functional in mice)
- ORs are **7-transmembrane GPCRs** most similar to rhodopsin
- Different odorants activate different **combinatorial patterns** of ORs → olfactory "code" analogous to visual color mixing but with ~400 dimensions

### Signal Transduction Cascade

1. Odorant binds OR on OSN cilia → **Gαolf** (olfactory-specific G protein alpha subunit) activated
2. Gαolf → stimulates **adenylyl cyclase III (ACIII)** → ↑**cAMP**
3. cAMP opens **CNG (cyclic nucleotide-gated) channels** → **Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ influx** → initial depolarization
4. **Amplification**: elevated Ca²⁺ activates **Ca²⁺-activated Cl⁻ channels (TMEM16B/anoctamin-2)** → Cl⁻ efflux → additional depolarization (Cl⁻ equilibrium potential more positive than resting potential in OSNs due to high intracellular Cl⁻)
5. Depolarization spreads to axon hillock → **action potential** → propagates along unmyelinated axon through cribriform plate to olfactory bulb
6. **Adaptation/termination**: calmodulin binds Ca²⁺ → reduces CNG channel sensitivity to cAMP; phosphodiesterase degrades cAMP; CNG channels close

### From Nose to Brain (CN I)

- OSN axons (unmyelinated, ~0.1–0.2 µm diameter) bundle in olfactory submucosa
- Form ~20 discrete fascicles (fila olfactoria) that pass through **foramina of the cribriform plate** of the ethmoid bone
- Enter the **anterior cranial fossa** and synapse in the **olfactory bulb**

> **Clinical**: Head trauma → shear forces at cribriform plate → sever CN I fascicles → **post-traumatic anosmia** (often permanent; no surgical repair possible for CN I).

## 🔑 Olfactory Bulb

### Organization

The olfactory bulb is a laminated CNS structure that performs the first central processing of olfactory signals:

| Layer (surface to deep) | Components | Function |
|------------------------|-----------|---------|
| Olfactory nerve layer | OSN axon terminals | Input from nose |
| **Glomerular layer** | **Glomeruli** (spherical neuropil, ~2,000/bulb); periglomerular cells (inhibitory, GABA/DA); external tufted cells | Critical convergence zone (see below) |
| External plexiform layer | Dendrites of mitral/tufted cells; granule cell processes | Lateral processing |
| Mitral cell layer | **Mitral cells** (large, principal output neurons) | Main output to cortex |
| Internal plexiform layer | Mitral/tufted cell axons; granule cell axons | Output routing |
| Granule cell layer | **Granule cells** (inhibitory interneurons, GABA; lack axon; dendrodendritic synapses with mitral cells) | Lateral inhibition; sharpening of odor representation; site of adult neurogenesis from SVZ |

### The Glomerulus: Convergence of OR Identity

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| All OSNs expressing the **same OR type** | Project to the **same 2 glomeruli** (one medial, one lateral) in each olfactory bulb |
| Convergence ratio | ~10,000 OSN axons → ~25 mitral cells per glomerulus (~400:1 or greater) |
| Result | Each glomerulus = "labeled line" for one OR type; spatial map of OR identity in olfactory bulb |
| Significance | Enormous signal amplification; olfactory bulb = spatial odor map (glomerular activity pattern = odor identity) |

### Lateral Inhibition in Olfactory Bulb

- **Periglomerular cells**: inhibit neighboring glomeruli → narrow the representation of odor around most-activated glomeruli
- **Granule cells**: form **dendrodendritic synapses** with mitral cell lateral dendrites; granule cells inhibit mitral cells → further sharpens tuning; also mediate centrifugal inhibition (feedback from piriform cortex)
- **Efferent fibers** from piriform cortex, anterior olfactory nucleus, and locus coeruleus modulate bulb processing (attention-dependent modulation of smell)

## 🔑 Central Olfactory Pathways

### Primary Olfactory Cortex (No Thalamic Relay)

Mitral/tufted cell axons exit olfactory bulb via the **olfactory tract**, which bifurcates at the **anterior perforated substance** into:

| Tract | Destination | Structure |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Lateral olfactory stria** (main) | **Primary olfactory cortex**: piriform cortex, periamygdaloid cortex, anterior cortical amygdaloid nucleus, entorhinal cortex (anterior portion) | 3-layer paleocortex; receives direct olfactory input WITHOUT thalamic relay |
| **Medial olfactory stria** (smaller) | Anterior olfactory nucleus; contralateral olfactory bulb via **anterior commissure** | Bilateral coordination; feedback to contralateral bulb |

> **Key fact**: The piriform cortex (prepiriform + periamygdaloid) = PRIMARY olfactory cortex — the only primary sensory cortex that is NOT neocortex (it is 3-layered paleocortex) and receives direct input WITHOUT thalamic relay.

### Secondary Olfactory Projections

| Structure | Route from Primary Cortex | Function |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------|
| **Amygdala** (basolateral + medial nuclei) | Direct from piriform cortex | Emotional valence of odors; fear conditioning to odors; sexual behavior; food aversion |
| **Hippocampus** | Via entorhinal cortex (parahippocampal gyrus) → perforant path | Olfactory memory (episodic and associative); Proustian memory (involuntary autobiographical recall triggered by smell) |
| **Hypothalamus** | Via medial forebrain bundle from amygdala/piriform | Feeding behavior; hormonal responses; autonomic responses to odors |
| **Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** | Via **dorsomedial nucleus of thalamus** (MDm) relay from piriform cortex | **Conscious odor perception**; hedonic quality (pleasant/unpleasant); flavor integration (taste + smell) |
| **Prefrontal cortex** | Via OFC and MDm | Odor working memory; attention-dependent modulation |

### Why Olfaction is the Most Emotionally Evocative Sense

- Direct projections to **amygdala** (emotional processing) and **hippocampus** (memory) WITHOUT thalamic filtering
- Visual, auditory, and somatosensory signals all pass through thalamic relay before reaching cortex — olfaction bypasses this gateway
- Result: olfactory stimuli can directly trigger emotional responses and autobiographical memories (Proustian recall — named after Marcel Proust's madeleine passage)

## 🔑 Special Topics

### Olfactory Coding and Discrimination

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Combinatorial coding | Each odorant activates a unique pattern of multiple ORs; each OR responds to multiple odorants → ~10¹² discriminable odors (estimated) |
| Intensity coding | Higher odorant concentration recruits more OSNs and activates more ORs → larger glomerular activation pattern |
| Olfactory adaptation | Prolonged exposure → reduced perception (receptor-level and central); accounts for habituation to ambient odors |
| Cross-adaptation | Structurally similar odorants may cross-adapt (share OR types) |

### Pheromones and the Vomeronasal Organ

| Feature | Human Status |
|---------|-------------|
| **Vomeronasal organ (VNO / Jacobson's organ)** | Present as vestigial pit in anterior nasal septum; lacks functional sensory neurons in adults |
| **Accessory olfactory bulb** | Absent in humans |
| **V1R and V2R receptor genes** | ~90% pseudogenized in humans |
| **Functional pheromone communication** | Debated; evidence for effects of androstenone, androstadienone on behavior/physiology but no definitive pheromone system identified |
| **MHC-related odor preferences** | Humans detect MHC (HLA) differences via body odor — possible mate selection relevance |

## 🏥 Clinical Significance

| Condition | Mechanism | Key Features |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Post-viral anosmia** | Damage to olfactory epithelium by viral infection; COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2) targets sustentacular cells (ACE2/TMPRSS2 expression) → OSN functional disruption (not OSN death directly) | Most common cause of acquired anosmia; COVID-19 associated parosmia (distorted smell) common; usually recovers weeks-months; maladaptive regeneration → parosmia |
| **Post-traumatic anosmia** | Frontal/occipital head impact → contrecoup → shear CN I fila at cribriform plate | Usually permanent; no repair possible; bilateral anosmia common; loss of flavor is major quality-of-life issue |
| **Foster Kennedy syndrome** | Olfactory groove or medial sphenoidal wing meningioma compressing ipsilateral CN I and optic nerve + raising ICP | **Ipsilateral anosmia + ipsilateral optic atrophy** (direct compression) + **contralateral papilledema** (raised ICP); triad is pathognomonic |
| **Kallmann syndrome** | Failure of GnRH neurons and olfactory axons to migrate from olfactory placode to brain (mutations: KAL1/anosmin, FGFR1, PROKR2, CHD7 among others) | **Congenital anosmia + hypogonadotropic hypogonadism** (absent puberty, infertility); X-linked (KAL1) or autosomal; aplastic/hypoplastic olfactory bulbs on MRI |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Alpha-synuclein Lewy bodies deposit in olfactory bulb and anterior olfactory nucleus in **Braak stages 1-2** (before substantia nigra involvement) | **Hyposmia is an early non-motor feature** predating motor symptoms by years; used as potential biomarker; Smell Identification Test (UPSIT) |
| **Alzheimer's disease** | Neurofibrillary tangles in entorhinal cortex → hippocampus; olfactory pathways affected early | Hyposmia; impaired odor identification; correlates with cognitive decline; early involvement of entorhinal cortex (which receives olfactory input) |
| **Phantosmia** | Hallucinated smell perception without external stimulus | Temporal lobe epilepsy (olfactory aura — often unpleasant "burning rubber," metallic); can also follow olfactory epithelium damage |
| **Parosmia** | Distorted smell (real odors perceived differently, usually unpleasantly) | Post-viral (COVID-19), post-traumatic; likely due to maladaptive OSN regeneration with miswiring of OR projections to glomeruli |
| **Ageusia vs. anosmia** | Most complaints of "taste loss" are actually anosmia — retronasal olfaction dominates flavor | Key history: can patient taste salt/sweet/sour on tongue? If yes, true taste intact, problem is olfactory |

### Olfactory Testing

| Test | Method | Use |
|------|--------|-----|
| **UPSIT (University of Pennsylvania Smell Identification Test)** | 40-item scratch-and-sniff multiple choice | Gold standard for hyposmia quantification; Parkinson's screening |
| **Sniffin' Sticks** | Threshold, discrimination, identification battery | European standard; TDI score |
| **Phenyl ethyl alcohol (PEA) threshold** | Pure olfactory stimulus (no trigeminal component) | Isolates olfactory from trigeminal (ammonia, menthol, capsaicin stimulate CN V too) |

# 👂 Auditory System

> The auditory system is a **mechanosensory system that transduces sound pressure waves (20–20,000 Hz in young adults) into neural signals via mechanosensitive hair cells in the cochlea**, then processes binaural auditory information through a series of brainstem nuclei with extensive bilateral projections before reaching the primary auditory cortex in the superior temporal plane.

## 🔗 Key Connections
- [Vestibular System](./anatomyLink/Nervous-System/Neuro-System-(Lippincott)/03 - Special Senses/Vestibular System.md) — shares CN VIII and labyrinthine apparatus
- [Brainstem](./anatomyLink/Nervous-System/Central-Nervous-System/Brainstem.md) — cochlear nuclei, superior olivary complex, inferior colliculus
- [Thalamus](./anatomyLink/Nervous-System/Neuro-System-(Lippincott)/04 - Brain Regions/Thalamus.md) — medial geniculate nucleus (MGN/MGB) as auditory relay
- [Cerebral Cortex](./anatomyLink/Nervous-System/Neuro-System-(Lippincott)/04 - Brain Regions/Cerebral Cortex.md) — primary auditory cortex (A1), Wernicke's area
- [Cranial Nerves](./anatomyLink/Nervous-System/Peripheral-Nervous-System/Cranial-Nerves.md) — CN VIII (vestibulocochlear), CN VII (stapedius reflex), CN V3 (tensor tympani)

## 🗂️ Key Information
| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Stimulus | Sound pressure waves (longitudinal compression waves in air) |
| Frequency range (human) | 20–20,000 Hz (best sensitivity 2,000–4,000 Hz) |
| Intensity range | 0 dB SPL (threshold) to ~120–140 dB SPL (pain) |
| Primary receptor cells | Inner hair cells (IHCs) — 3,500; Outer hair cells (OHCs) — 12,000 |
| Cranial nerve | CN VIII (cochlear division of vestibulocochlear nerve) |
| Thalamic relay | Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN), medial geniculate body |
| Primary cortex | A1 (Heschl's gyrus), superior temporal plane, Brodmann areas 41/42 |
| Key feature vs. vision | Extensive **bilateral** projections at brainstem level (partial decussation) |

## 🔑 The Ear: Structural Overview

### Three Compartments

| Division | Structures | Function |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **External ear** | Pinna (auricle) → external auditory canal (EAC, ~2.5 cm) → tympanic membrane | Sound collection, direction cues (pinna shape), resonance of EAC (~3,400 Hz) |
| **Middle ear** | Ossicular chain (malleus → incus → stapes → oval window), Eustachian tube, air-filled tympanic cavity | **Impedance matching**: air → fluid transition; muscles: tensor tympani, stapedius |
| **Inner ear** | Cochlea (hearing) + vestibular labyrinth (balance); housed in petrous temporal bone | Mechanotransduction; frequency analysis |

### Middle Ear: Impedance Matching

Without the middle ear, ~99.9% of sound energy would be reflected at the air-fluid interface. The middle ear amplifies pressure by ~×22–44:

| Mechanism | Contribution |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Area ratio** of tympanic membrane to oval window (~17:1) | ~25 dB pressure gain |
| **Lever action** of malleus/incus | Additional ~3 dB |
| **Buckling effect** of tympanic membrane | Minor additional gain |

### Middle Ear Muscles

| Muscle | Nerve | Reflex | Function |
|--------|-------|--------|---------|
| **Tensor tympani** | CN V3 (medial pterygoid branch) | Trigeminovagal (jaw movement, etc.) | Pulls malleus medially; stiffens TM; minimal acoustic reflex role in humans |
| **Stapedius** | CN VII (facial nerve, stapedial branch) | **Acoustic (stapedius) reflex** — loud sounds >70–90 dB | Pulls stapes posteriorly; stiffens ossicular chain; attenuates low frequencies; protects cochlea from self-generated sounds (activated before speaking) |

> **Clinical**: CN VII paralysis (Bell's palsy) → **hyperacusis** (painful sensitivity to sound) due to lost stapedius reflex.

## 🔑 The Cochlea

### Three Fluid-Filled Compartments (Scala)

| Compartment | Fluid | Composition | Connection |
|-------------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Scala vestibuli** | Perilymph | High Na⁺, low K⁺ (like ECF) | Begins at oval window |
| **Scala media (cochlear duct)** | Endolymph | **High K⁺ (~150 mM), low Na⁺** — unique; produced by stria vascularis | Separated from scala vestibuli by Reissner's membrane; from scala tympani by basilar membrane |
| **Scala tympani** | Perilymph | High Na⁺, low K⁺ | Ends at round window |

> **Endocochlear potential**: stria vascularis (on outer wall of scala media) maintains a +80 mV potential in endolymph relative to perilymph — this is the driving force for K⁺ influx into hair cells during stereocilia deflection (total driving force = ~150 mV: −70 mV resting membrane potential + +80 mV endocochlear potential).

### Tonotopy: Basilar Membrane Frequency Coding

| Cochlear Location | Basilar Membrane Properties | Frequency Tuning |
|-------------------|----------------------------|-----------------|
| **Base** (near oval window) | Narrow (~0.1 mm), stiff | **High frequencies** (>4,000 Hz, up to 20,000 Hz) |
| **Apex** (helicotrema) | Wide (~0.5 mm), floppy/compliant | **Low frequencies** (<500 Hz) |
| Mid-cochlea | Intermediate | Mid-range (1,000–4,000 Hz) |

> **Von Békésy's traveling wave**: sound sets up a traveling wave along the basilar membrane with a peak at the characteristic frequency location of each hair cell — the mechanical basis of tonotopy.

### Organ of Corti

The organ of Corti sits on the basilar membrane within the scala media:

| Cell Type | Number | Arrangement | Innervation | Function |
|-----------|--------|------------|-------------|---------|
| **Inner hair cells (IHCs)** | ~3,500 | Single row (pillar cell side) | ~95% of afferent CN VIII fibers (type I spiral ganglion neurons) | **Primary auditory transducers** — detect basilar membrane motion → signals to brain |
| **Outer hair cells (OHCs)** | ~12,000 | 3 rows (lateral) | ~5% of afferent CN VIII fibers (type II); receive abundant efferent (olivocochlear bundle) | **Cochlear amplifier** — active electromotility via **prestin** (motor protein in lateral membrane); sharpen frequency tuning, amplify soft sounds up to 40–60 dB |
| **Supporting cells** | Various | Pillar, Deiters, Hensen, Claudius cells | — | Structural support, K⁺ recycling |
| **Tectorial membrane** | Gelatinous membrane above | Covers stereocilia | — | Shear force on OHC stereocilia; IHC stereocilia deflected by fluid movement |

### Mechanotransduction in Hair Cells

1. Basilar membrane moves upward → **stereocilia deflect toward tallest stereocilium (kinocilium-direction)**
2. **Tip links** (cadherin-23 / protocadherin-15) between adjacent stereocilia are stretched → pull open **MET (mechanotransduction) channels** (TMC1/TMC2)
3. **K⁺ (and Ca²⁺) rush in** from endolymph (driven by +80 mV endocochlear potential)
4. Hair cell **depolarizes** → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels at basolateral membrane open → Ca²⁺ influx → **glutamate release** onto type I spiral ganglion neurons
5. K⁺ exits via basolateral channels → recycled back to endolymph via supporting cells → stria vascularis
6. Deflection away from tallest stereocilium → tip links slack → MET channels close → hyperpolarization → ↓glutamate

## 🔑 Auditory Pathway

### From Cochlea to Cortex

| Structure | Location | Key Features |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Spiral ganglion (CN VIII)** | Modiolus of cochlea | Type I (IHC input, 90–95%) and Type II (OHC input, 5–10%); enters brainstem at pontomedullary junction |
| **Cochlear nuclei (CN + DCN)** | Medulla (pontomedullary junction) | First central auditory synapse; **ALL cochlear nerve fibers synapse here**; tonotopic; ventral CN (AVCN + PVCN) and dorsal CN (DCN) have distinct response properties |
| **Superior olivary complex (SOC)** | Caudal pons | **First binaural integration**: LNTB/MSO process ITD (interaural time differences — low freq); LSO processes ILD (interaural level/intensity differences — high freq); origin of **olivocochlear efferents** |
| **Lateral lemniscus** | Lateral pons/midbrain | Ascending pathway; nucleus of lateral lemniscus adds processing |
| **Inferior colliculus (IC)** | Midbrain tectum | **All ascending auditory pathways converge here**; tonotopic laminar organization; important for auditory reflexes, sound localization, temporal processing |
| **Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN/MGB)** | Posterior thalamus | Three divisions: ventral (tonotopic, lemniscal), dorsal (multimodal), medial (diffuse); relay to auditory cortex |
| **Primary auditory cortex (A1)** | Heschl's gyrus (transverse temporal gyrus), superior temporal plane | Brodmann areas 41/42; tonotopically organized; responds to pure tones; in left hemisphere: adjacent Wernicke's area for speech comprehension (area 22) |

### Binaural Processing: Sound Localization

| Cue | Mechanism | Frequency Range | Processing Site |
|-----|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Interaural time difference (ITD)** | Microsecond delay between ears | Low frequency (<1,500 Hz) | Medial superior olive (MSO) — coincidence detector using delay lines (Jeffress model) |
| **Interaural level difference (ILD)** | Intensity difference between ears (due to head shadow) | High frequency (>1,500 Hz) | Lateral superior olive (LSO) — excitatory-inhibitory computation |
| **Spectral cues** | Pinna shape modifies spectrum (elevation, front/back) | All frequencies | Higher cortical areas |

### Key Feature: Bilateral Projections
Unlike the visual system (complete contralateral segregation below cortex), auditory pathways project **bilaterally** at every level from the superior olivary complex upward. This means:
- **Unilateral cortical lesions cause only mild, ipsilateral hearing deficits** (easily missed on clinical testing)
- **Bilateral cortical lesions** → cortical deafness (auditory agnosia)
- Brainstem lesions → **central hearing loss** (both ears affected) + other brainstem signs

## 🔑 Hearing Loss: Types and Assessment

### Tuning Fork Tests

| Test | Conductive Loss | Sensorineural Loss |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Weber** (512 Hz fork on vertex) | Lateralizes to **affected (bad) ear** (ambient noise excluded from affected side → IHC receives more bone conduction signal) | Lateralizes to **unaffected (good) ear** |
| **Rinne** (512 Hz fork, mastoid vs. ear canal) | **BC > AC** (bone conduction better than air conduction) — abnormal | **AC > BC** (normal pattern, but both reduced) |

### Types of Hearing Loss

| Type | Site of Lesion | Characteristics | Examples |
|------|---------------|----------------|---------|
| **Conductive** | External or middle ear | Air-BC gap on audiogram; Weber to bad ear; Rinne BC > AC | Cerumen impaction, otitis media with effusion, otosclerosis, TM perforation |
| **Sensorineural (SNHL)** | Cochlea, CN VIII, or central | No air-BC gap; Weber to good ear; Rinne AC > BC but reduced | Presbycusis, noise-induced, Menière's, acoustic neuroma, ototoxic drugs |
| **Mixed** | Both | Features of both on audiogram | Chronic otitis media with ossicular erosion + cochlear damage |
| **Central** | Brainstem/cortex | Pure-tone thresholds may be normal; difficulty with speech in noise, localization | Brainstem stroke, auditory cortex lesions |

### Audiogram Patterns

| Pattern | Likely Diagnosis |
|---------|----------------|
| High-frequency sloping loss | Presbycusis (age-related; OHC loss at base of cochlea) |
| 4 kHz notch | Noise-induced hearing loss (OHC most vulnerable at 4 kHz due to resonance properties) |
| Low-frequency fluctuating loss | Menière's disease |
| Flat conductive loss | Otosclerosis, effusion |
| Unilateral SNHL + tinnitus | Acoustic neuroma |

## 🏥 Clinical Significance

| Condition | Pathophysiology | Key Features | Treatment |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Otosclerosis** | Abnormal endochondral bone remodeling → stapes footplate fixation → impaired vibration | Conductive hearing loss (usually bilateral); autosomal dominant tendency; worse in pregnancy; Carhart's notch on audiogram (~2 kHz dip in bone conduction) | Stapedectomy/stapedotomy with prosthesis; hearing aids |
| **Acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma)** | Benign Schwann cell tumor of CN VIII (vestibular division, superior more than inferior) | Unilateral SNHL + tinnitus + vertigo; cerebellopontine angle (CPA) mass; bilateral = NF2 (neurofibromatosis type 2, chromosome 22q, merlin protein) | Surgery (translabyrinthine, retrosigmoid), stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife), observation |
| **Menière's disease** | Endolymphatic hydrops — distension of endolymph compartment, mechanism unclear | Episodic: fluctuating low-frequency SNHL + roaring tinnitus + ear fullness + rotatory vertigo (20 min–4 hr attacks); eventually permanent SNHL | Dietary (low salt, low caffeine), diuretics, intratympanic steroids/gentamicin; endolymphatic sac surgery |
| **Presbycusis** | Age-related cochlear hair cell loss (base first → high frequencies first) + strial atrophy + neural loss | Gradual symmetric high-frequency SNHL; most common cause of hearing loss in adults; starts >50 y/o | Hearing aids; cochlear implant if severe |
| **Noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL)** | OHCs most vulnerable; 4 kHz notch on audiogram (cochlear resonance); metabolic exhaustion, oxidative stress, mechanical disruption | Temporary (TTS) vs permanent (PTS) threshold shift; NIOSH: 85 dBA TWA 8 hr limit | Prevention (hearing protection); irreversible once permanent |
| **Ototoxicity** | Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin, streptomycin — cochleotoxic and vestibulotoxic), cisplatin (cochleotoxic, basal hair cells), loop diuretics (furosemide — stria vascularis), aspirin (reversible tinnitus + SNHL) | High-frequency SNHL; tinnitus; monitor with serial audiograms during treatment | Prevention; cochlear implant if severe |

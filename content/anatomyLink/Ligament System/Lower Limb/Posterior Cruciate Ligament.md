# Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL)

> The **posterior cruciate ligament (PCL)** is the **strongest ligament in the knee**, running from the posterior intercondylar area of the tibia to the medial femoral condyle. It prevents **posterior tibial translation**.

## 🔗 Key Connections
- **Origin** — Posterior intercondylar area of [Tibia](./anatomyLink/Skeletal-System/Lower-Limb/Tibia.md) (posterior to tibial spines)
- **Insertion** — Lateral surface of **medial femoral condyle** ([Femur](./anatomyLink/Skeletal-System/Lower-Limb/Femur.md))
- **Two bundles** — Anterolateral (larger, taut in flexion) + Posteromedial (taut in extension)
- **Related structures** — [Anterior Cruciate Ligament](./anatomyLink/Ligament-System/Lower-Limb/Anterior-Cruciate-Ligament.md), menisci, [Femur](./anatomyLink/Skeletal-System/Lower-Limb/Femur.md), [Tibia](./anatomyLink/Skeletal-System/Lower-Limb/Tibia.md)

## 🏥 Clinical Significance
- **PCL tear** — Dashboard injury (knee flexed, tibia pushed posterior); posterior "sag" of tibia
- **Posterior sag sign** — Tibia sags posteriorly when knee held at 90° flexion
- **Posterior drawer test** — Posterior tibial translation at 90° flexion
- **PCL reconstruction** — Less common than ACL; often managed conservatively if isolated tear
- **Dial test** — Increased external tibial rotation at 30° (PLC) vs 90° (PCL + PLC) indicates injury level
